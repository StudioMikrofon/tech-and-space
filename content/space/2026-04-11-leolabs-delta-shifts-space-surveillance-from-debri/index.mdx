Space domain awareness has long focused on one existential risk: collisions. [LeoLabs](https://leolabs.space), a company built on mapping orbital debris with radar precision, now explicitly targets another—**intentional interference**. Their new tool, **Delta**, marks the first commercial product designed to flag adversarial maneuvers in orbit, not just accidental ones.


The shift is subtle but seismic. Until now, LeoLabs’ [public-facing platform](https://platform.leolabs.space) provided collision warnings for satellite operators, a service grounded in transparency. Delta, by contrast, is **military-exclusive**, its capabilities described only in broad strokes: identifying ‘unusual patterns’ that suggest hostile intent. No specifics on what constitutes a ‘pattern,’ no examples of detected incidents—just the quiet implication that space is no longer a passive domain.


This aligns with a larger trend. The U.S. Space Force’s [2023 budget](https://www.spaceforce.mil/News/Article/3249220/space-force-fy-2023-budget-proposal-focuses-on-resiliency-and-integration/) earmarked $1.3 billion for ‘tactical ISR’—intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance in orbit. Delta isn’t a government program, but it’s the first commercial tool positioning itself as a force multiplier for those efforts. The question isn’t whether space is militarized, but how deeply private companies will embed in that infrastructure.

<ArticleBreak />

The scientific community has watched this evolution with measured concern. While collision avoidance relies on shared data (e.g., [NASA’s CSpOC](https://www.space-track.org) feeds), adversarial tracking thrives on secrecy. Delta’s closed-loop design—data in, alerts out, no public audit—raises questions about **false positives** in an environment where misinterpretation could escalate tensions. A ‘suspicious maneuver’ to one operator might be a routine station-keeping burn to another.


LeoLabs hasn’t disclosed whether Delta integrates with existing military systems like the [Space Surveillance Network](https://www.spacecom.mil/SSN), or if it operates as a standalone layer. That ambiguity matters. If Delta’s algorithms flag an anomaly, who verifies it? The tool’s value proposition—**speed**—could become its liability if unchecked alerts trigger unnecessary responses.


For now, the company frames this as an extension of its [space safety mission](https://leolabs.space/blog/space-safety). Yet the pivot to defense applications suggests a calculated bet: that governments will pay premiums for proprietary insights into orbital behavior. The real test isn’t technical, but geopolitical. Will Delta’s users treat it as a **deterrent** or a **provocation**?
