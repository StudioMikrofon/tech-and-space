Cancer drug development has a well-documented flaw: when a target like [PD-1](https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/treatment/types/immunotherapy/pd-1-pd-l1-blockade) or [HER2](https://www.cancer.gov/types/breast/hp/breast-treatment-pdq#section/_163) shows promise, the field rushes in, saturating the space with incremental variants. Stipple Bio’s $100 million launch—backed by [ARCH Venture Partners](https://www.archventure.com/) and [8VC](https://8vc.com/)—is a calculated rejection of that model. Instead of competing in overcrowded arenas, the company is mapping less-explored regions of cancer proteins, where precision could outpace the noise of me-too therapies.


The approach hinges on a simple but radical premise: most protein targets in oncology are either ignored or poorly understood. Aaron Ring, a Yale immunobiologist and Stipple co-founder, has spent years dissecting how [antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41573-023-00721-2) bind to surface proteins—work that suggests even well-studied targets like HER2 have untapped binding sites. Stipple’s platform, still in stealth mode, appears to combine high-throughput screening with structural biology to identify these ‘dark matter’ regions.


This isn’t about finding entirely new proteins. It’s about redefining what a ‘druggable’ site looks like on ones we already know.

<ArticleBreak />

The timing is deliberate. After a decade of [checkpoint inhibitors](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/resources-information-approved-drugs/fda-approves-pembrolizumab-advanced-melanoma) and [HER2-targeted therapies](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2203690) dominating pipelines, the low-hanging fruit has been picked clean. Stipple’s bet is that the next wave of breakthroughs will come from **subtargets**—specific epitopes or conformational states that competitors have missed. Early signals suggest their first programs will focus on solid tumors, where [tumor heterogeneity](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrc.2016.112) makes broad-spectrum approaches less effective.


Yet the strategy carries risks. Precision demands deeper validation: a target that’s overlooked might be overlooked for good reason. The company’s [Series A announcement](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20231012005203/en/Stipple-Bio-Launches-with-100-Million-Series-A-Financing-to-Advance-Next-Generation-Cancer-Therapies) emphasizes ‘unprecedented selectivity,’ but selectivity alone doesn’t guarantee efficacy. The real test will be whether these subtargets translate to durable responses in patients—or if they’re just another layer of complexity in an already crowded field.


For now, Stipple’s silence on specific programs speaks volumes. In an industry where [preclinical hype](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S016777992100123X) often outpaces clinical reality, their focus on structural precision over flashy targets is a quiet rebellion.

Operationally, Stipple’s approach could force a reckoning for ADC developers. If subtargeting proves viable, the cost of entry for competing in ‘crowded’ spaces like HER2 will rise—no longer will a me-too antibody suffice. The bottleneck won’t be target selection; it’ll be resolution.
