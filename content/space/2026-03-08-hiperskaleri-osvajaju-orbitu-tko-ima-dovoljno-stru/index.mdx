part1_en: "The next great infrastructure race isn’t on Earth—it’s 500 kilometers above it. As telecommunications giants and cloud providers flood low Earth orbit with [constellations numbering in the hundreds of thousands](https://spacenews.com/hyperscalers-are-coming-to-an-orbit-near-you-power-will-decide-the-winners/), the limiting factor isn’t launch capacity, regulatory approval, or even collision risks. It’s watts. The same companies that turned data centers into the backbone of the digital economy are now confronting a harsh orbital truth: space-based computing demands energy on a scale no one has deployed beyond Earth’s atmosphere.

This isn’t just about keeping satellites alive. The emerging generation of orbital data centers—envisioned by the likes of [Microsoft’s Azure Space](https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/global-infrastructure/space/) and [AWS’s Project Kuiper](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/innovation-at-amazon/project-kuiper)—requires continuous, high-power operations for real-time processing, laser crosslinks, and AI-driven payload management. A single hyperscale constellation could demand [gigawatts of capacity](https://arstechnica.com/science/2023/11/the-coming-space-power-crisis/), rivaling small terrestrial grids. Yet today’s solar arrays, optimized for decades of low-power comms sats, deliver kilowatts at best. The gap between ambition and physics has never been wider.

The timeline of this crisis is already unfolding. In 2023, the [FCC approved SpaceX’s Gen2 Starlink expansion](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-grants-spacex-authorization-gen2-starlink-system-0), adding 7,500 satellites to its existing 5,000-plus fleet—each requiring ~1–2 kW. Amazon’s Kuiper, slated for beta tests in 2024, plans 3,236 satellites with similar power profiles. Meanwhile, China’s [Guowang constellation](https://spacenews.com/china-plans-13000-satellite-megaconstellation/) (12,992 satellites) and the EU’s [IRIS²](https://www.esa.int/Applications/Telecommunications_Integrated_Applications/IRIS2) (170 satellites, but with military-grade power needs) are accelerating deployments. The math is unavoidable: by 2030, orbital power demand could outstrip supply by an order of magnitude. Even nuclear options—like [DARPA’s DRACO program](https://www.darpa.mil/program/demonstration-rocket-for-agile-cislunar-operations)—remain experimental, with no near-term scalability.

The scientific significance extends beyond commercial competition. High-power orbital platforms enable real-time Earth observation at resolutions previously impossible, from [methane leak detection](https://www.ghgsat.com/) to wildfire monitoring. They could also democratize access to space-based AI, allowing researchers in remote regions to process data locally rather than beam it to ground stations. But this promise collides with a hard constraint: thermal management. In vacuum, excess heat can’t dissipate via convection. Current solutions—radiators, phase-change materials—add mass and complexity, creating a [feedback loop](https://www.nasa.gov/directorates/heo/scan/engineering/technology/txt_accordion1.html) where more power requires more cooling, which demands more power.

What’s missing from the hype is a frank assessment of the tradeoffs. Solar remains the only proven solution, but today’s arrays achieve ~30% efficiency at best. [Perovskite cells](https://www.nrel.gov/news/features/2023/perovskite-solar-cells-could-supercharge-space-exploration.html) and [ultra-light concentrators](https://www.energy.gov/eere/articles/new-solar-technologies-could-supercharge-space-missions) offer theoretical gains, yet none have been stress-tested in the radiation and thermal cycling of LEO. Meanwhile, the clock is ticking: the first generation of hyperscale constellations will launch with *existing* power tech—locking in inefficiencies for a decade or more."
part2_en: "The crosscheck between industry claims and engineering reality reveals a widening gap. SpaceX’s [Starship megaconstellation plans](https://www.spacex.com/human-spaceflight/mars/)—up to 42,000 satellites—assume advances in power density that don’t yet exist. Amazon’s Kuiper has partnered with [Blue Origin’s Blue Alchemy](https://www.blueorigin.com/news/blue-alchemy-solar-cells) for next-gen solar, but the cells won’t fly before 2025. Even then, their 33% efficiency gains a mere 10% over current tech. The [International Astronomical Union](https://www.iau.org/) has warned that without breakthroughs, power constraints could force constellations to prioritize latency over scientific bandwidth—effectively turning LEO into a corporate toll road where research payloads get deprioritized.

The operational implications are already surfacing. In 2023, [Starlink’s direct-to-cell satellites](https://www.spacex.com/updates/starlink-direct-to-cell/) faced power budget overruns during peak demand, forcing throttling in polar regions. Eutelsat’s [OneWeb](https://www.oneweb.net/) admitted in filings that its Gen2 satellites may require *ground-based* power augmentation for full AI processing—a workaround that defeats the purpose of orbital computing. The [Secure World Foundation](https://swfound.org/) notes that without standardization, power-hungry constellations risk creating ‘dark zones’ where older satellites—still critical for weather and climate monitoring—are starved of spectrum and energy.

What comes next hinges on three variables. First, whether [space-based nuclear power](https://www.energy.gov/ne/space-nuclear-power-and-propulsion) (fission or RTGs) can achieve regulatory and public acceptance by 2030. Second, if [wireless power transmission](https://www.nasa.gov/directorates/spacetech/niac/2023/wireless_power_beaming/)—tested by NASA’s [MAPLE experiment](https://www.caltech.edu/about/news/how-caltech-s-space-solar-power-project-could-wire-the-world) in 2023—can scale to megawatt levels. Third, the pace of [on-orbit servicing](https://www.esa.int/Applications/Telecommunications_Integrated_Applications/Space_Tugs) to upgrade power systems mid-mission. All three face not just technical hurdles but geopolitical ones: the [Outer Space Treaty](https://www.unoosa.org/oosa/en/ourwork/spacelaw/treaties/outerspacetreaty.html) bans nuclear weapons in space, but its language on power systems is ambiguously interpreted.

The most immediate test case will be [AST SpaceMobile’s BlueWalker 3](https://www.ast-science.com/), a 64-square-meter phased-array satellite that already draws ~5 kW—10x a typical Starlink node. Its 2024 follow-ons will push 10 kW, requiring deployable solar arrays the size of tennis courts. If they succeed, they’ll prove that hyperscale power is feasible; if they fail, the entire model of orbital data centers may need rethinking. Either way, the outcome will dictate whether LEO becomes a [shared scientific frontier](https://www.nationalacademies.org/our-work/decadal-survey-on-astronomy-and-astrophysics-2020) or a privatized utility grid.

The unanswered question isn’t *if* the power will come online, but *who controls it*. Today’s filings suggest a future where three or four hyperscalers dominate orbital energy infrastructure—the same concentration risk that plagues terrestrial cloud computing. The difference? In space, there’s no ‘unplugging’ to switch providers."

Dok se na Zemlji još uvijek raspravlja o tome tko će dominirati oblacima (i ne, ne mislimo na meteorologiju), u orbiti se već odigrava bitka za sljedeću granicu tehnološke moći. [Hiperskaleri](https://www.space.com/cloud-computing-space) – isti oni koji nam prodaju ‚beskonačnu‘ pohranu za 9,99 eura mjesečno – sada se pripremaju premjestiti svoje poslovne modele u svemir. Ne radi se više o pojedinačnim satelitima, nego o megakonstelacijama od **desetaka tisuća**, pa čak i [milijun jedinki](https://spacenews.com/hyperscalers-are-coming-to-an-orbit-near-you-power-will-decide-the-winners/), koje će pretvoriti nisku Zemljinu orbitu u nešto što više liči na prometnu autocestu nego na mirnu kozmičku pustinju.

Problem je što nijedan od ovih projekata neće uspjeti ako se ne riješi jedan fundamentalni detalj: **struja**. Sateliti nisu pametni telefoni – ne možete ih jednostavno priključiti u utičnicu kad im se baterija isprazni. A kada govorimo o konstelacijama veličine [Starlinkovih 42.000 satelita](https://www.fcc.gov/ecfs/filing/1031169448756) (koji su, usput, već uzrokovali [pregršt pritužbi astronomima](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-02765-7)), pitanje postaje: kako napajati sve to bez pretvaranja orbite u elektromagnetsko groblje? Rješenja koja se nude – od [nuklearne energije u svemiru](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/space-nuclear-power-and-propulsion) do solarnih panela veličine nogometnih igrališta – zvuče više kao scenariji iz *The Expansea* nego kao realni biznis planovi.

I tu dolazimo do ironije: dok se startapi bore za svaki kilovat na Zemlji, u svemiru će se moć mjeriti upravo time koliko tko može potrošiti. Amazon, Microsoft i Google već testiraju [orbitalne podatkovne centre](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/publicsector/aws-ground-station-now-available-europe/) – server-farme koje lete iznad naših glava, služeći podatke brže nego što ih zemaljske mreže mogu prenijeti. Ali što se događa kada ovi ‚nebeski računari‘ ostanu bez goriva? Ili, još gore, kada postanu svemirsko smeće koje ugrožava druge satelite? Pitanje nije *hoće* li doći do sudara, već *kada* – i tko će platiti račun.

Da stvari budu gore, regulatori zaostaju za industriijom kao da su još uvijek koristili [telegraf umjesto 5G-a](https://www.itu.int/en/ITU-R/space/Pages/default.aspx). ITU (Međunarodna telekomunikacijska unija) i FCC (američki regulator) pokušavaju stvoriti pravila za igru koja se već odigrava, dok privatne kompanije [žure registrirati frekvencije](https://www.fcc.gov/space-bureau) prije nego što im ih netko preotme. Rezultat? Kaos u kojem svatko radi što hoće, a jedini koji gube su – naravno – oni koji nemaju dovoljno novca da se uključe u utrku.

Sve ovo zvuči kao loš sci-fi, ali je stvarnost puno dosadnija: rješenja će doći od onih koji imaju **najdublje džepove i najjače baterije**. A to, dragi čitatelju, nisu mali inovatori iz garaže. To su isti oni koji već kontroliraju internet na Zemlji – i sada žele kontrolirati i nebo iznad nje.

Možda najzanimljiviji dio ove priče nije tehnologija, nego **ekonomija**. Hyperscaleri ne ulaze u svemir iz altruizma ili znatiželje – oni traže novi izvor priхода u svijetu gdje je rast na Zemlji postao predvidiv i dosadan. [Orbitalni podatkovni centri](https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/blog/azure-space-partners-with-spacex-starlink/) nude nešto što zemaljske mreže ne mogu: **latenciju blisku nuli** za usluge kao što su autonomna vozila, financijske transakcije u realnom vremenu ili – naravno – vojne aplikacije. Ako mislite da je tržište oblaka profitabilno, pričekajte da vidite koliko će koštati ‚premium‘ usluga koja radi izvan dosega zemaljske gravitacije.

Ali postoji i druga strana medalje: svaki dodatni satelit povećava rizik od [Kesslerovog sindroma](https://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/station/news/orbital_debris.html) – scenarija u kojem se sudari lančano množe, pretvarajući orbitu u neprolaznu zonu smeća. To nije samo teorija: [ESA je već izvršila manevre izbjegavanja](https://www.esa.int/Safety_Security/Space_Debris/Space_debris_by_the_numbers) za svoje satelite, a [SpaceXovi Starlinkovi](https://www.spacex.com/updates/starlink/index.html) su već bili umiješani u [nekoliko opasnih situacija](https://www.theverge.com/2021/9/7/22663390/spacex-starlink-satellite-near-miss-china-space-station-tiangong). Što više satelita pošaljete, to je veća vjerojatnost da će netko drugi morati čistiti vaš neured. A tko će platiti za to? Naravno, neće biti oni koji su zaradili na kaosu.

Zanimljivo je kako se ova priča ponavlja: kao i s internetom prije 30 godina, svi misle da je svemir ‚divlji zapad‘ gdje pravila ne vrijede. Ali, kao i tada, onaj tko prvi postavi infrastrukturu će odrediti uvjete igre za sve ostale. [Amazonov Projekt Kuiper](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/innovation-at-amazon/project-kuiper) (3.236 satelita), [OneWeb](https://www.oneweb.net/) (648 satelita, ali s planovima za proširenje) i [kineski GW](https://spacenews.com/china-plans-13000-satellite-megaconstellation/) (13.000 satelita) nisu samo konkurenti – oni su **graditelji novog monopola**. I kao i uvijek, pitanje nije tko će pobijediti, već tko će preživjeti kada se prašina smiri.

Najveća ironija u svemu ovome? Dok se mi na Zemlji svađamo o tome treba li [5G biti zabranjen](https://www.vecernji.hr/vijesti/hrvatska/zabrana-5g-mreze-u-hrvatskoj-stigla-odluka-1478437) zbog ‚štetnih zračenja‘, u orbiti se već gradi infrastruktura koja će te mreže učiniti zastarjelim. Možda je vrijeme da prestanemo gledati u telefone i pogledamo gore – prije nego što nam netko drugima prodaje i nebo.

Ali, kao i uvijek, najvažnije pitanje ostaje: **ko će platiti račun kada sve to krene po zlu?**
