When the European Space Agency’s [Juice probe](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Space_Science/Juice) unfurled its solar arrays last year—the largest ever built for an interplanetary mission—it carried a silent admission: even our best photovoltaics are a compromise. Rigid, heavy, and brittle, conventional silicon panels are ill-suited for the demands of deep space, where every gram counts and deployment must survive years of radiation, temperature swings, and mechanical stress.

Enter perovskite solar cells. The Dutch research organization [TNO](https://www.tno.nl/) has now launched [Perovion Technologies](https://www.pv-magazine.com/2024/02/20/tno-launches-perovion-technologies-to-industrialize-perovskite-pv/), a spinout tasked with building the world’s first roll-to-roll manufacturing facility for lightweight, flexible perovskite panels by 2030. The target isn’t rooftops—it’s the niches where silicon stumbles: foldable arrays for CubeSats, conformal skins for lunar rovers, or even deployable power systems for Martian habitats.

The move is less about replacing silicon than supplementing it. Perovskites, a class of materials with a crystal structure that excels at absorbing light, have long tantalized researchers with their potential for high efficiency at a fraction of the weight. Lab cells have hit [over 33% efficiency](https://www.nrel.gov/pv/cell-efficiency.html) in tandem with silicon—better than silicon alone—but scaling up has been the hurdle. Roll-to-roll production, a method borrowed from printing and flexible electronics, could finally bridge that gap. If successful, Perovion’s factory would mark the first time perovskites graduate from lab curiosities to industrial workhorses.

Yet the real test isn’t efficiency—it’s endurance. Silicon panels degrade slowly and predictably; perovskites, while improving, still face questions about longevity under [prolonged UV exposure](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsenergylett.1c02567) and thermal cycling. The 2030 timeline isn’t just about construction. It’s about proving whether these cells can last long enough to matter in space, where replacement isn’t an option.

<ArticleBreak />

The mission context here is broader than one factory. NASA’s [Artemis program](https://www.nasa.gov/artemis) has already flagged power generation as a critical bottleneck for sustained lunar operations, and the ESA’s [Moonlight initiative](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Space_Science/Moonlight) aims to create a permanent lunar communication and navigation network—both of which will demand power sources that are light, flexible, and resilient. Perovskites, if they meet durability thresholds, could slot into these plans as a complementary technology.

What’s often overlooked in the hype around perovskites is their *operational* advantage. A roll-to-roll process doesn’t just cut costs; it enables form factors silicon can’t match. Imagine solar cells integrated into the fabric of inflatable habitats or wrapped around the curved surfaces of a [SpaceX Starship](https://www.spacex.com/vehicles/starship/)’s cargo bay. The Dutch facility’s focus on niche applications is a strategic hedge: it avoids direct competition with entrenched silicon manufacturers while targeting markets where flexibility and weight savings justify higher upfront costs.

Still, uncertainties remain. The [International Roadmap for Photovoltaics](https://www.itrpv.net/) notes that perovskite stability under real-world conditions is still an open question, particularly for space applications where repair is impossible. Perovion’s roadmap includes rigorous testing, but the 2030 deadline is as much about answering these questions as it is about scaling production.

The factory’s success—or failure—will also serve as a litmus test for Europe’s ability to translate materials science into industrial leadership. While China and the U.S. dominate silicon production, perovskites represent a rare opportunity for Europe to carve out a niche in next-generation photovoltaics. The [Netherlands’ history](https://www.tno.nl/en/about-tno/our-history/) in precision manufacturing (from ASML’s lithography machines to Philips’ early solar research) suggests the infrastructure exists. Whether the patience does is another matter.
