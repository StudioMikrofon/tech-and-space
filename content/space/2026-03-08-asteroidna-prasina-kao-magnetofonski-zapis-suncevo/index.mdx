When Japan’s [Hayabusa-2 probe](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Space_Science/Hayabusa2) returned to Earth in 2020 with 5.4 grams of asteroid Ryugu tucked inside its sample capsule, scientists knew they were holding something rare: a direct fragment of the solar system’s infancy. Unlike Earth, whose geology has been endlessly rewritten by plate tectonics and erosion, Ryugu’s dust and rock have remained largely untouched since the Sun’s planetary disk coalesced 4.6 billion years ago. The magnetic properties of these grains, in particular, promised to act as a fossilized recording—like cosmic tape hissing with the echoes of the young Sun’s magnetic field.

  Yet when multiple research teams began analyzing the same samples, their results diverged. Some detected strong magnetic signatures suggesting Ryugu’s parent body had once hosted a dynamo-driven core; others found only faint traces, implying the asteroid’s magnetism was acquired later, from external sources like solar wind or micrometeorite impacts. The discrepancies weren’t just academic quibbles. If Ryugu’s magnetism originated internally, it would mean its progenitor was large enough—and hot enough—to separate molten metal from silicate, a critical step in planet formation. If external, it would rewrite assumptions about how magnetic fields permeated the early solar system.

  A [new study published in *Journal of Geophysical Research: Planets*](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2024JE008321) now argues the conflict stems from a deceptively simple issue: sample size. The teams analyzing Ryugu’s grains were working with milligram-scale fragments, each potentially capturing a different moment in the asteroid’s history. "It’s like trying to reconstruct a symphony from a handful of notes," said lead author [Benjamin Weiss](https://eapsweb.mit.edu/people/benweiss), a planetary scientist at MIT. "Some notes might belong to the first movement, others to the coda." The paper’s statistical reanalysis suggests the varying results aren’t contradictions but complementary—each a snapshot of Ryugu’s complex magnetic past.

&lt;ArticleBreak />

The findings underscore a broader challenge in planetary science: how to extrapolate global truths from microscopic evidence. Hayabusa-2’s mission was designed to mitigate this by collecting samples from two distinct sites on Ryugu, yet even that proved insufficient for magnetic studies. "We’re dealing with materials that have been battered, heated, and altered over billions of years," noted [Yuki Kimura](https://www.kobe-u.ac.jp/en/NEWS/research/2023_03_20_01.html), a co-author from Kobe University. "A single grain’s history might not represent the whole asteroid’s."

  Mission context complicates the picture further. Hayabusa-2 was never intended as a magnetic survey; its primary goal was to test sample-return technology and analyze Ryugu’s organic chemistry. The magnetic data emerged as a bonus—a testament to how modern instruments can squeeze unexpected science from limited material. Yet this serendipity comes with caveats. Without a dedicated magnetometer on the spacecraft itself (unlike NASA’s [OSIRIS-REx](https://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/osiris-rex), which carried one to Bennu), researchers rely entirely on lab-based measurements of returned samples. That leaves gaps: Were the grains magnetized in space, or did their properties change during re-entry or Earth-side handling?

  What’s next is clearer. The study’s authors call for two parallel efforts: first, reanalyzing existing Ryugu samples with standardized protocols to reduce inter-lab variability; second, prioritizing magnetic instrumentation on future asteroid missions. NASA’s [Psyche mission](https://www.jpl.nasa.gov/missions/psyche), en route to a metal-rich asteroid, will test magnetometers in deep space, while JAXA’s [MMX mission](https://global.jaxa.jp/projects/sat/mmx/) to Phobos could provide comparative data from a moon that may have accreted differently. For now, Ryugu’s conflicting signals serve as a reminder: the solar system’s earliest chapters are written in faint, fragmented ink. Deciphering them requires patience—and more than a few grains of dust.
