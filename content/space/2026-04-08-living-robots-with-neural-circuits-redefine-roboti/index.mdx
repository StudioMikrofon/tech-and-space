Engineers have crossed a biological threshold: robots built not from metal or code, but from living cells. Published last month in *Advanced Science*, the research introduces "neurobots"—tiny, self-directed systems where neurons wire themselves into functional circuits. Unlike traditional robots that mimic biology, these constructs *are* biology, assembling into free-swimming forms with no mechanical skeleton or AI brain.


The breakthrough represents a fundamental shift in robotics. For decades, engineers have pursued biologically inspired designs—AI modeled on brains, quadrupeds mimicking dogs—but this work skips imitation entirely. Instead, it harnesses living cells to create machines that grow, adapt, and organize themselves. The implications extend beyond novelty: these neurobots offer a platform to study how simple neural networks produce complex behaviors, a question central to both neuroscience and future cyborg systems.


The scale of these constructs is deliberately small, designed to explore cellular-level computation. Early experiments show neurons forming spontaneous connections, though the exact mechanisms remain under study. What’s clear is that this isn’t robotics as we know it. It’s a new category of machine—one that blurs the line between organism and tool.

<ArticleBreak />

The scientific significance lies in its potential to bridge biology and engineering. Neurobots could help researchers understand how neural circuits develop *in vivo* while avoiding the ethical and technical challenges of animal testing. They may also pave the way for hybrid systems, where living neural networks interface with synthetic components—a step toward true cyborgs. Yet, the field is still in its infancy. Questions about scalability, control, and long-term viability remain unanswered.


For now, the neurobots serve as a proof of concept: living cells can be programmed to perform tasks without external guidance. This suggests a future where machines aren’t just inspired by life but *are* life, built from the ground up. The work aligns with broader trends in synthetic biology, where scientists are increasingly treating cells as programmable units. If successful, such systems could revolutionize fields from regenerative medicine to environmental sensing.


The shift from bio-inspired to bio-constructed robotics also raises ethical questions. How should we classify machines composed of living cells? What rights or safeguards might they require? The IEEE Robotics community has already noted the need for frameworks to address these concerns, signaling a debate that will intensify as the technology advances.

For exploration, the question isn’t whether living robots will become mainstream, but what boundaries they’ll force us to redraw. Will they remain tools for scientific study, or could they evolve into something more? The answer may redefine the line between machine and organism altogether.
