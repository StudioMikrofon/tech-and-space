On May 29, 2024, Planet Labs—a company whose constellation of 200+ satellites images the entire Earth daily—[quietly updated its usage policy](https://www.planet.com/pulse/updates-to-our-acceptable-use-policy/) to prohibit customers from analyzing "battle damage assessment" in active conflict zones. The change, buried in legalese, marks a turning point: the era when commercial Earth observation was purely a tool for science, agriculture, or climate monitoring is over. Now, it is also a controlled instrument of war.

The policy explicitly bars users from leveraging Planet’s high-resolution imagery (down to 30–50 cm per pixel) to track military strikes, assess infrastructure destruction, or monitor troop movements in regions like Ukraine, Gaza, or Sudan. While the company frames this as a neutral act—"preventing misuse by adversarial actors"—the move underscores a harsh reality: [satellite data has become a dual-use technology](https://www.armscontrol.org/act/2023-04/features/commercial-satellite-imagery-new-frontier-arms-control), as critical to modern warfare as GPS or encrypted communications. Planet’s decision didn’t emerge in a vacuum. It follows years of escalating tensions over how open-access imagery enables both humanitarian accountability and military targeting. In 2022, [Ukrainian forces used Planet and Maxar data](https://www.theverge.com/2022/3/3/22959310/ukraine-russia-war-satellite-imagery-maxar-planet-labs) to document Russian war crimes in Bucha; by 2023, analysts noted that the same imagery could help adversaries refine artillery strikes or evade sanctions.

The scientific community has watched this shift with unease. Planet’s data underpins research on deforestation, glacial retreat, and urban heat islands—work that relies on unfettered access to global imagery. But the company’s new restrictions, while legally defensible, create a precedent: commercial operators now act as de facto arbiters of what can be seen, by whom, and for what purpose. "This isn’t just about one policy," noted [Dr. Laura Grego of the Union of Concerned Scientists](https://www.ucsusa.org/resources/satellite-imagery-and-war), in a recent briefing. "It’s about whether Earth observation remains a public good—or becomes another privatized layer of the battlefield."

The timing of Planet’s policy update is telling. It arrives as the U.S. government [tightens export controls](https://www.commerce.gov/news/press-releases/2024/04/commerce-department-imposes-new-export-controls-advanced-computing-and) on satellite technology, citing national security risks, and as private firms like SpaceX’s Starlink face scrutiny for their role in conflicts. Yet Planet’s move is distinct: unlike Starlink’s active denial of service (e.g., [limiting Ukrainian drone strikes](https://www.reuters.com/technology/spacex-limited-ukraines-use-starlink-drones-says-new-elon-musk-biography-2024-04-05/)), this is a preemptive restriction on *analysis*—not data collection itself. The imagery still exists; the question is who gets to interpret it.

For researchers, the implications are immediate. Projects tracking environmental damage in war zones—such as [oil spills in Yemen](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S004896972104836X) or burned croplands in Sudan—now risk violating terms of service if their work indirectly intersects with military activity. Planet has offered no public criteria for what constitutes "battle damage assessment," leaving scientists to guess where the line lies between, say, documenting a dam collapse (permitted) and noting its proximity to a barracks (prohibited). The [Committee on Earth Observation Satellites (CEOS)](https://ceos.org/), a consortium of 60+ space agencies, has yet to issue guidance, though insiders describe "intense" closed-door discussions.

What’s next is a test of whether commercial remote sensing can retain its scientific integrity while operating in a militarized landscape. Planet’s competitors—like [Maxar](https://www.maxar.com/) (now owned by private equity) and [BlackSky](https://www.blacksky.com/)—have yet to follow suit, but the pressure is mounting. The U.S. National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA) already [purchases 90% of its imagery from commercial providers](https://www.nga.mil/MediaRoom/PressReleases/2023/Documents/NGA-Commercial-Imagery-Strategy-2023.pdf); as conflicts proliferate, the line between civilian and military use will blur further. The alternative—a return to the pre-2010s era, when only governments controlled high-resolution imagery—is unthinkable for climate science. Yet the current path risks turning every pixel into a potential weapon.

The deeper tension lies in the asymmetry of access. While Planet’s policy applies uniformly, its enforcement won’t. Well-funded intelligence agencies or defense contractors can afford custom licensing or proprietary alternatives; academic researchers and NGOs cannot. "We’re entering an era where the most detailed views of Earth are available," said [Dr. Moriba Jah of the University of Texas](https://www.astronomy.com/science/space-exploration/2024/03/space-traffic-management), "but only if you’re on the right side of a paywall—or a geopolitical divide."
