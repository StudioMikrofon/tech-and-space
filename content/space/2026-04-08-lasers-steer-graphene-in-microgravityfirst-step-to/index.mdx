A parabolic flight over Bordeaux last month did more than simulate weightlessness—it proved a laser’s ability to maneuver graphene aerogels with measurable force in microgravity. Researchers from [Dresden University of Technology](https://tu-dresden.de) and collaborators mounted a 20-milligram graphene aerogel sample inside an aircraft performing 31 parabolas, each offering 22 seconds of near-zero gravity. During these windows, a 1-watt laser directed at the aerogel generated thrust equivalent to 1.2 millinewtons per square meter—enough to demonstrate controlled movement without physical contact.


The experiment, detailed in a preprint for [*Nature Communications*](https://www.nature.com/ncomms/), wasn’t about raw power but precision. Graphene’s ultra-low density (just 0.18 milligrams per cubic centimeter) and high light absorption made it an ideal candidate for laser propulsion, a concept first proposed for solar sails but never before tested in realistic microgravity conditions. Unlike chemical thrusters or ion drives, this method requires no fuel, only a directed energy source—a critical advantage for long-duration missions where mass constraints dominate.


What distinguishes this from earlier ground tests is the elimination of air resistance and convection, two variables that previously masked the laser’s true effect. In the aircraft’s free-fall phases, the team observed the aerogel’s acceleration align almost perfectly with theoretical models, validating a decade of computational predictions. The implications stretch beyond propulsion: if scalable, this could enable fine adjustments to a satellite’s orientation or orbit using nothing but light from a ground station or another spacecraft.

The timeline here is deliberate. This wasn’t a spontaneous discovery but the culmination of a [three-year EU-funded project](https://cordis.europa.eu/project/id/828975) to test graphene’s viability in space environments. Earlier phases focused on material stability under vacuum and thermal cycling; the parabolic flight marked the first dynamic test. According to project lead [Prof. Stefanie Kroker](https://tu-dresden.de/ing/elektrotechnik/ife/optoelektronik/die-professur/mitarbeiter/akademische-mitarbeiter/stefanie-kroker), the next step is a suborbital rocket test in 2025, where the team will attempt to sustain propulsion for up to six minutes—long enough to simulate a low-Earth orbit insertion burn.


Yet the path from lab to launchpad remains steep. Current laser systems lack the power density to replace conventional thrusters for heavy payloads, and graphene aerogels must still prove their durability against cosmic radiation and micrometeoroids. The [European Space Agency’s](https://www.esa.int) Advanced Concepts Team has expressed cautious interest but notes that operational use would require laser arrays orders of magnitude more powerful than today’s prototypes.


The real bottleneck may not be the technology itself but the infrastructure to deploy it. Ground-based laser stations capable of tracking and propelling satellites would need adaptive optics to compensate for atmospheric distortion—a challenge already being tackled by projects like the ESA’s [Optical Ground Station in Tenerife](https://www.esa.int/Our_Activities/Space_Engineering_Technology/TeraNet). For now, the focus remains on niche applications: station-keeping for CubeSats or debris removal, where even millinewton-level forces could extend mission lifetimes by years.

The responsible question isn’t whether lasers will propel spacecraft tomorrow, but where this fits in the hierarchy of near-term needs. Does the space community prioritize perfecting photon-based thrust for precision tasks, or does it first solve the harder problem of scaling laser power? And if graphene proves fragile in orbit, what backup materials—like [molybdenum disulfide](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.nanolett.1c00797)—might take its place?
