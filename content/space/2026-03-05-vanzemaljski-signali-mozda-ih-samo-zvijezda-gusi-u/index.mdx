For six decades, the search for extraterrestrial intelligence has hinged on a fundamental assumption: that an advanced civilization would broadcast narrowband radio signals—precise, focused, and distinct from the natural static of the cosmos. These signals, like a laser beam cutting through fog, should stand out against the broad-spectrum noise of stars, pulsars, and interstellar plasma. But a [new study](https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.3847/1538-4357/acf85e) from researchers at the SETI Institute, published in *The Astrophysical Journal*, suggests that the universe may be far less cooperative than we hoped. Stellar activity—what scientists call 'space weather'—could be distorting these hypothetical transmissions before they ever reach our telescopes, spreading their energy across a wider band of frequencies and rendering them indistinguishable from the background hum.

The mechanism is deceptively simple. When a radio signal passes through the turbulent plasma near a star—especially a young, magnetically active one like our Sun in its infancy—the signal’s path bends and scatters. This effect, known as [multipath propagation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multipath_propagation), is familiar to engineers designing terrestrial communications systems, where it can degrade GPS or cellular signals. But in the context of interstellar distances, the consequences are more profound. A transmission that left its source as a razor-sharp spike in the radio spectrum could, by the time it reaches Earth, be smeared into a broad, weak smear—one that slips through the filters of traditional narrowband searches like [SETI’s Allen Telescope Array](https://www.seti.org/ata) or the [Breakthrough Listen](https://breakthroughinitiatives.org/initiative/1) project.

This isn’t just theoretical. The study’s authors, led by SETI Institute researcher [Dr. Sofia Sheikh](https://www.seti.org/people/sofia-sheikh), modeled how signals from exoplanets orbiting young, flare-prone stars (like the [TRAPPIST-1 system](https://exoplanets.nasa.gov/trappist1/)) would fare under such conditions. Their simulations showed that even a highly directed, continuous transmission could be broadened by orders of magnitude—enough to evade detection unless observers knew *exactly* where and when to look. The implication is unsettling: we may have already missed signals not because they weren’t sent, but because the stars themselves conspired to hide them.

The findings arrive at a critical juncture for SETI. After decades of scanning the skies with increasingly sensitive instruments, the field has yet to detect a confirmed technosignature. Some researchers have begun to question whether the [narrowband assumption](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.06847) is too restrictive—whether we’ve been searching for a needle in a haystack while ignoring the possibility that the needle might have dissolved into the hay. This study adds weight to that skepticism, suggesting that the haystack itself is far more dynamic, and far more hostile to detection, than previously thought.

The challenge now is to adapt. Traditional SETI strategies rely on flagging signals that are *narrow* in frequency and *continuous* in time—characteristics that distinguish artificial transmissions from natural phenomena like [fast radio bursts](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-020-1195-4) or solar flares. But if stellar plasma can blur these signatures, the search parameters may need to expand. ‘We’re not saying these signals are undetectable,’ Sheikh clarifies in a [statement](https://www.seti.org/press-release/stellar-activity-could-make-extraterrestrial-signals-harder-detect) accompanying the paper. ‘We’re saying we need to look differently.’

One approach is to prioritize stars with calmer magnetic fields—older, more stable systems where plasma turbulence is less severe. Another is to develop algorithms that can reconstruct smeared signals by modeling the distorting effects of interstellar plasma, a technique already used in [pulsar timing arrays](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06212-2) to correct for interstellar scattering. The [Very Large Array](https://public.nrao.edu/telescopes/vla/) and the upcoming [Square Kilometre Array](https://www.skatelescope.org/) could play a key role here, offering the sensitivity and computational power to sift through broader bands of noise for patterns that might betray an artificial origin.

Yet the study also raises a deeper question: if stellar activity can obscure signals, could it do the same for *our* transmissions? Earth’s own radio leakage—from military radars, television broadcasts, and deep-space probes—has been spreading into the galaxy for nearly a century. But if a civilization around a young, active star were listening, would they hear us? Or would our signals, too, be lost in the static? The answer depends on the star’s age, the frequency of our broadcasts, and the luck of alignment. For now, the only certainty is that the cosmos is a noisier, more chaotic place than the early SETI pioneers imagined.

The findings come as SETI enters a new phase of collaboration with astrobiology and exoplanet research. Projects like [NASA’s Habitable Worlds Observatory](https://asd.gsfc.nasa.gov/hwo/) aim to characterize the atmospheres of Earth-like planets, while SETI efforts increasingly focus on ‘technosignatures’ beyond radio—such as [laser pulses](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-021-01508-x) or atmospheric pollutants. Yet radio remains the most practical medium for interstellar communication, and this study underscores that its challenges are not just technological but *astrophysical*. The universe, it seems, is not a passive stage for our searches—it’s an active participant, shaping what we can and cannot see.

What’s next? The SETI Institute plans to test these models against real data, using the [Allen Telescope Array to observe known exoplanet systems](https://www.seti.org/ata-observations) during periods of high stellar activity. If the predictions hold, it could prompt a shift in how we design future searches—perhaps even a return to broader-band surveys, which fell out of favor in the 1990s due to computational limits. ‘The good news,’ Sheikh notes, ‘is that we’re learning how to account for these effects. The bad news is that it makes the search even harder.’
