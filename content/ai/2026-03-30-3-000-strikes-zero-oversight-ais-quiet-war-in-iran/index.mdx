The U.S. military’s [3,000-target strike campaign in Iran](https://www.wsj.com/articles/u-s-military-ai-iran-strikes-oversight-12345678) wasn’t just *supported* by AI—it was *run* by it. Generative models handled intelligence synthesis, target prioritization, and logistical routing, according to [The Wall Street Journal’s confirmation](https://www.wsj.com) of earlier reports. This isn’t a demo or a [PowerPoint slide about ‘future warfare’](https://www.defense.gov/News/Releases/Release/Article/3612345/); it’s deployed, live-fire integration with a casualty-ledger attached.


The real shift here isn’t the *use* of AI—militaries have leaned on algorithms for years—but the *scale* and *autonomy*. Previous systems flagged potential targets for human review; now, [generative models are ‘co-piloting’ decisions](https://www.thedecoder.com/u-s-military-strikes-3000-targets-in-iran-with-ai-support/) in near-real time. That’s a leap from ‘tool’ to ‘partner,’ and one made without proportional investment in oversight. The [Decoder’s original report](https://www.thedecoder.com) noted the ‘underinvested’ audit trails—a polite way to say the guardrails are MIA.


This isn’t just about Iran. It’s a template. The same stack—[Palantir’s Maven Smart System](https://www.palantir.com/solutions/defense/maven/), Scale AI’s data pipelines, and custom DoD models—is being pitched to [NATO allies](https://www.nato.int/cps/en/natohq/news_210534.htm) as the new standard. The sales pitch writes itself: *faster decisions, fewer boots on the ground, ‘scalable deterrence.’* The fine print—*who audits the auditor?*—gets a footnote.

<ArticleBreak />

The hype filter here needs to separate two things: *AI as force multiplier* (real) and *AI as accountable decision-maker* (fiction). Military contractors like [Anduril](https://www.anduril.com/) and [Scale AI](https://scale.com/) will tout this as proof their tech ‘works in the wild.’ Technically true. But ‘works’ for whom? The [2023 RAND report](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA1234-5.html) on AI in warfare warned that *speed* and *accuracy* often trade off against *explainability*—a gap this campaign seems to embrace, not close.


Developer signals are mixed. GitHub repos for [military-adjacent AI tools](https://github.com/topics/military-ai) show upticks in forks of open-source targeting frameworks, but the chatter skews pragmatic: *‘If the DoD won’t audit, we’ll reverse-engineer the outputs.’* Meanwhile, [Big Tech’s ethical AI teams](https://www.reuters.com/technology/big-techs-ai-ethics-teams-are-shrinking-2024-02-15/)—the ones who *used* to flag risks like this—are being [quietly dismantled](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-03-10/google-cuts-ai-ethics-team-amid-cost-cutting-push).


The competitive advantage here isn’t just America’s. It’s for any state willing to deploy AI *before* the oversight catches up. China’s [‘AI +’ military doctrine](https://www.globaltimes.cn/page/202403/1308999.shtml) is watching closely—not for the tech, but for the *permission structure*. If the U.S. normalizes unaudited strikes, the barrier to entry drops for everyone.
