Zodasiran, the latest small interfering RNA to target ANGPTL3, just cleared its phase 1 basket trial with a familiar script: lower triglycerides in severe hypertriglyceridemia, and a bonus LDL drop in heterozygous familial hypercholesterolemia (HeFH) patients. The results, published in [Nature Medicine](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04307-8), arrive nearly a decade after the first ANGPTL3 inhibitors hit the scene—raising the question of whether this is a genuine therapeutic leap or just another repackaging of an old target.


The numbers look solid on paper: patients with severe hypertriglyceridemia saw meaningful triglyceride reductions, while HeFH patients got a two-for-one deal with LDL cholesterol dips. But here’s the catch—ANGPTL3 inhibition isn’t new. Regeneron’s evinacumab, a monoclonal antibody targeting the same pathway, already won FDA approval in 2021 for homozygous familial hypercholesterolemia. The difference? Zodasiran is an RNA therapy, which could mean longer-lasting effects and fewer infusions. That’s the pitch, anyway.


Still, the phase 1 data only covers short-term outcomes. Long-term safety, durability, and real-world adherence remain open questions. RNA therapies have a habit of looking promising in early trials, only to stumble when scaled—just ask the failed PCSK9 RNA candidates that never made it past phase 2. The hype filter here is simple: if zodasiran can’t prove it’s more than just a longer-acting evinacumab, it’ll join the growing pile of "me-too" lipid drugs.

<ArticleBreak />

The competitive landscape is already crowded. Statins, PCSK9 inhibitors, and now evinacumab dominate the lipid-lowering market, leaving little room for another player unless it brings something truly novel. Zodasiran’s RNA mechanism could be that edge—if it delivers on the promise of less frequent dosing. But RNA therapies come with their own baggage: delivery challenges, immune responses, and manufacturing complexities that have sunk similar candidates before.


Developer signals are muted so far. No GitHub repositories or open-source chatter about zodasiran, which isn’t surprising given its early-stage status. The real test will come when Arrowhead Pharmaceuticals (the drug’s developer) releases phase 2 data. If the results hold, it could pressure Regeneron to accelerate its own RNA-based ANGPTL3 programs—or risk ceding ground in the race for next-gen lipid therapies.


For now, the industry is watching, but not betting. The lipid drug market is littered with promising phase 1 results that never panned out. Zodasiran’s real competition isn’t evinacumab—it’s the graveyard of failed RNA therapies that came before it. The question isn’t whether it works, but whether it’s worth the hype.

The real signal here is Arrowhead’s gamble on RNA over antibodies. If zodasiran succeeds, it could shift the lipid therapy landscape toward longer-acting treatments. If it fails, it’s back to the drawing board for RNA-based lipid drugs.
