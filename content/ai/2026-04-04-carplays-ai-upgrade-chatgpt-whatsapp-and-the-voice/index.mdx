Apple’s CarPlay finally admitted what we all knew: voice assistants in cars are still terrible. This week’s update—[ChatGPT](https://openai.com/blog/chatgpt), [Google Meet](https://workspace.google.com/products/meet/), and [Audiomack](https://www.audiomack.com/)—doesn’t fix that, but it at least stops pretending Siri is the answer. ChatGPT’s integration, live since iOS 16.4, is the headliner: a voice-only interface where you bark questions at a dashboard and pray the mic picks it up over road noise. No typing, no scrolling—just a list of conversation titles (if you’re lucky) and a robotic voice reading responses.


The real test isn’t whether ChatGPT *works* in CarPlay, but whether it’s better than asking your passenger to Google something. Early signals suggest it’s a lateral move: useful for trivia, useless for anything requiring context. Meanwhile, WhatsApp’s beta—currently limited to testers—ditches the old Siri-based ‘send message to [contact]’ workflow for something closer to actual messaging. No word yet on whether it’ll support voice notes or read receipts, because apparently we’re still treating car screens like 2010 feature phones.


Google Meet’s arrival is the quietest win here: a functional video call interface (audio-only, obviously) that doesn’t require juggling a phone mid-drive. Audiomack, the streaming underdog, gets a seat at the table by default—proof that CarPlay’s app approval process remains as opaque as ever.

<ArticleBreak />

The industry map here is simple: Apple cedes ground to third parties while its own [CarPlay Ultra](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2023/06/apple-previews-next-generation-of-carplay/)—the ‘take over your entire dashboard’ play—stalls in a single [Aston Martin](https://www.astonmartin.com/en). Google and OpenAI gain a foothold in the one place Apple’s ecosystem was supposed to be untouchable. WhatsApp’s upgrade, if it ships, could finally make CarPlay messaging tolerable, though Meta’s track record on car integrations ([remember MirrorLink?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MirrorLink)) suggests caution.


Developers aren’t exactly cheering. The lack of public APIs for CarPlay’s new voice features means this is still a walled garden—apps get in if Apple says so, and good luck debugging mic issues. GitHub chatter focuses on [workarounds for voice command latency](https://github.com/topics/carplay-voice), not breakthroughs. And let’s not forget: none of this fixes CarPlay’s core flaw—it’s still a phone projection, not a native car OS.


The real signal isn’t the apps themselves, but the timing. Apple’s [WWDC](https://developer.apple.com/wwdc24/) is weeks away, and CarPlay’s ‘big’ update is a handful of third-party integrations. If this is the warm-up act, the main event better involve actual maps that don’t reroute you into a lake.
