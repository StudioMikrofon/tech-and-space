But according to *Wired*’s reporting, the Pentagon had already been kicking the tires on Microsoft’s Azure-hosted OpenAI models *before* the policy change, testing tools for everything from [intelligence analysis to cyber operations](https://www.wired.com/story/pentagon-openai-microsoft-ai-military/). So much for principles leading practice.

  The timing is either a masterclass in bureaucratic foresight or a textbook case of policy as post-hoc justification. OpenAI’s [original 2023 ban](https://archive.ph/2023.01.10-2023.01.10-https://openai.com/policies/usage-policies) on military use was never absolute—it carved out exceptions for 'national security' work—but the Pentagon’s reported experiments suggest the line between 'defense' and 'warfare' was always more permeable than the marketing implied. Microsoft, meanwhile, has been [openly courting the DoD](https://news.microsoft.com/2023/09/12/microsoft-expands-partnership-with-u-s-department-of-defense/) for years, positioning Azure as the default cloud for AI-powered 'decision advantage.' The real question isn’t whether OpenAI’s models are now cleared for combat adjacency (they are), but whether this was ever a *technical* decision—or just a commercial one in slow-motion PR drag.

  Let’s be clear: this isn’t about some sudden leap in AI capability. The models in question are the same [GPT-4 and custom Azure variants](https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/blog/advancing-ai-in-the-department-of-defense-with-azure-openai-service/) the Pentagon could’ve accessed indirectly via Microsoft’s existing contracts. The 'change' is purely about OpenAI’s willingness to stamp its logo on the workflow. That’s not trivial—symbolism matters in Silicon Valley’s arms race for ethical high ground—but it’s a far cry from the breathless framing of AI as a 'new domain of warfare.' (Spoiler: [automated summarization of drone footage](https://www.c4isrnet.com/artificial-intelligence/2023/11/01/pentagon-turns-to-ai-to-help-analyze-drone-video/) is not Skynet.)

  The more interesting signal here is the **hype filter** at work. OpenAI’s policy shift was rolled out with the usual [blog-post-as-manifesto](https://openai.com/blog/preparing-for-the-future) fanfare, emphasizing 'democratic input' and 'aligned deployment.' Yet the Pentagon tests—if *Wired*’s sources are accurate—hint at a classic pattern: the most sensitive customers get early access under NDAs, while the public debate lags behind as damage control. It’s the same playbook Big Tech uses for [cloud contracts](https://www.nextgov.com/it-modernization/2023/07/how-microsoft-and-aws-are-competing-pentagons-10-billion-cloud-contract/132563/) and [facial recognition](https://www.theverge.com/2020/6/11/21284683/ibm-facial-recognition-analysis-software-police-surveillance-rekognition-amazon-ai): first the deals, then the ethics panels.

  So what’s actually new? Not the tech. Not even the use case. What’s new is OpenAI’s willingness to **trade its carefully cultivated 'responsible AI' brand for a seat at the defense table**—and Microsoft’s role as the enabler-in-chief. The real benchmark here isn’t model performance; it’s how much ethical squirming a company will tolerate for a slice of the DoD’s [$1.8 trillion budget](https://comptroller.defense.gov/Portals/45/Documents/defbudget/fy2024/FY2024_Budget_Request_Overview_Book.pdf)."
part2_en: "The **industry map** here is straightforward: Microsoft wins. Again. While OpenAI grapples with the [fallout from its non-profit-to-corp pivot](https://www.technologyreview.com/2023/11/20/1083560/openai-microsoft-sam-altman-ai/) and [developer backlash](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=38345678) over closed models, Azure OpenAI Service becomes the default conduit for Pentagon AI—locking in a lucrative, sticky customer. Google, which [dropped its 'no military AI' stance in 2021](https://www.theverge.com/2021/10/13/22722605/google-cloud-military-contracts-artificial-intelligence-ai-pentagon), is playing catch-up. Amazon, with its [classified cloud regions](https://aws.amazon.com/government-education/government/classified/), is the quiet third wheel.

  For OpenAI, the calculus is riskier. The company has spent years positioning itself as the [thoughtful counterweight](https://openai.com/blog/our-approach-to-ai-safety) to reckless AI deployment. Now it’s in the awkward position of arguing that [summarizing intelligence reports](https://www.defenseone.com/technology/2023/11/pentagon-turns-ai-help-analyze-drone-video/170023/) is *fundamentally different* from, say, targeting recommendations—a distinction that will sound like hair-splitting to critics. The **developer signal** is already mixed: while some GitHub threads [dismiss the news as inevitable](https://github.com/orgs/community/discussions/56789), others point to the [growing chasm](https://www.theregister.com/2023/11/15/openai_developers_anger/) between OpenAI’s open-source roots and its closed-door defense deals. 'If it’s good enough for the Pentagon, why isn’t it good enough for indie devs?' [asked one commenter](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=38350121), capturing the tension in a sentence.

  The **reality gap** between demo and deployment is where this gets interesting. Military AI isn’t about flashy generative text—it’s about [logistics, maintenance, and ISR (intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance)](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RR4498.html). The models being tested are likely fine-tuned for [structured data extraction](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06983) and [anomaly detection](https://www.darpa.mil/program/artificial-intelligence-explorations), tasks where OpenAI’s models are competent but not uniquely superior to [open-source alternatives](https://huggingface.co/blog/llm-leaderboard) like Mistral or Llama. The Pentagon’s interest isn’t a vote of confidence in GPT-4’s superiority; it’s a vote of confidence in Microsoft’s ability to [deliver compliant, air-gapped deployments](https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/solutions/government/).

  What’s missing from the hype is the **benchmark context**: these tools are being tested against decades-old [rule-based systems](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-19-499) and human analysts, not some sci-fi baseline. Early adopters in the [Defense Innovation Unit](https://www.diu.mil/) have [noted](https://www.c4isrnet.com/artificial-intelligence/2023/04/20/pentagon-officials-say-ai-is-ready-for-prime-time/) that AI’s biggest value isn’t autonomy but *augmentation*—reducing the cognitive load on overworked staff. That’s a far cry from the [autonomous weapons panic](https://www.un.org/disarmament/autonomous-weapons/) that dominates headlines. The Pentagon isn’t betting on AI to win wars; it’s betting on AI to [keep the lights on](https://www.defenseone.com/technology/2023/09/pentagon-wants-ai-help-fix-its-broken-supply-chains/389522/) in an era of shrinking budgets and expanding threats.

  The unanswered question is whether OpenAI’s policy shift is a one-off accommodation for a key partner (Microsoft) or the first step toward a broader embrace of defense work. If it’s the latter, the company will need to explain why its models are safe for [JEDI cloud contracts](https://www.nextgov.com/it-modernization/2023/07/microsoft-wins-22-billion-pentagon-cloud-contract/388565/) but not, say, [predictive policing](https://www.theverge.com/2023/4/19/23690462/ai-predictive-policing-crime-forecasting-algorithms-bias). That’s a distinction even the most charitable observer will struggle to square."

Kada je OpenAI 2019. godine objavio svoja pravila korištenja, među prvim točkama bilo je jasno: *nema vojne primjene*. Ne za oružje, ne za obavještajnu analitiku, ne za bilo što što bi moglo završiti u rukama [Ministarstva obrane SAD-a](https://www.defense.gov/). To je, naravno, trajalo sve dok se nije pokazalo nepraktično – odnosno do siječnja 2024., kada je tvrtka tiho [izmijenila uvjete](https://openai.com/policies/usage-policies) i dopustila „ograničenu“ vojnu upotrebu. Problem? Prema izvješćima [Wireda](https://www.wired.com/story/openai-military-ban-pentagon-microsoft-azure/), Pentagon je već godinu dana prije toga, putem Microsoftovog Azure Government clouda, mirno testirao OpenAI-ove modele. Bez javne debate, bez transparentnosti, bez ikakvog objašnjenja kako se to podudara s tadašnjim pravilima.

Ne čudi da je upravo Microsoft u središtu ove priče. Tvrtka je u OpenAI uložila [13 milijardi dolara](https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2023/01/23/microsoft-extends-partnership-with-openai/) (2023.), dobivši ekskluzivna prava na integraciju njihovih modela u svoje usluge – uključujući [Azure Government](https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/solutions/government/), platformu namijenjenu upravo vladinim i vojnim korisnicima. Azure Government nije običan cloud: certificiran je za *FedRAMP High* i *DoD Impact Level 5*, što znači da može rukovati podacima do razine *tajne*. Drugim riječima, Microsoft je već imao sve potrebno da OpenAI-ove modele ponudi Pentagonu – čak i kada je OpenAI službeno zabranjivao takvu upotrebu.

Ovdje se nameće pitanje: tko je zapravo odlučivao? OpenAI je možda imao pravila, ali je Microsoft imao ugovore – i [dugogodišnju suradnju s vojskom](https://news.microsoft.com/2021/03/31/microsoft-wins-21-88-billion-u-s-army-contract-for-augmented-reality-headsets/), uključujući onaj slavni [21,9 milijardi dolara](https://www.defense.gov/News/News-Stories/Article/Article/2537193/army-selects-microsoft-to-deliver-augmented-reality-headsets/) za AR naočale za vojsku. Ako ste Microsoft, a imate platformu koja je već odobrena za vojnu upotrebu, zašto bi vas zaustavilo nekoliko točaka u OpenAI-ovim uvjetima korištenja? Posebno kada ti isti uvjeti nisu bili dio vašeg vlastitog ugovora s korisnicima.

Još zanimljivije je što niti OpenAI ni Pentagon nisu potvrdili o kojem točno testiranju se radi. Da li su modeli korišteni za logistiku, kiberobranu, ili nešto treće? [Wiredovi izvori](https://www.wired.com/story/openai-military-ban-pentagon-microsoft-azure/) ostaju anonimni, a službene izjave – nepostojeće. Jedino što znamo je da je OpenAI *nakon* navodnog testiranja promijenio pravila, dopuštajući vojnu upotrebu *uz iznimke* (npr. „ne za razvoj oružja“ ili „štetu ljudima“). Čudna slučajnost, zar ne?

Da je ovo bila izolirana priča o jednoj tvrtki koja je prekršila vlastita pravila, možda bi se moglo proći olako. Ali ovdje se radi o sistemskom problemu: tehnološki divovi i vlade već godinama igraju igru *prvo uradi, pa pitaj za dopuštenje*. Microsoft nije prvi put u takvoj situaciji – [2019. godine](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/10/25/technology/microsoft-military-contract.html) su zaposlenici protestirali zbog ugovora s vojskom, ali je tvrtka nastavila s poslovima kao da se ništa nije dogodilo. OpenAI, s druge strane, već godinaima balansira između idealizma svojih osnivača i komercijalnih interesa svojih ulagača. [Sam Altman](https://www.theverge.com/2023/11/21/23971282/openai-sam-altman-fired-ceo-board-microsoft-satya-nadella) je još 2023. govorio o „odgovornom AI“-ju, dok je u pozadini tvrtka već radila na načinu kako opustiti restrikcije za profitabilnije klijente.

Što se tiče pravne sive zone, ovdje je sve jasno: Microsoftovi uvjeti korištenja Azurea vjerojatno nisu eksplicitno zabranjivali vojnu upotrebu OpenAI-ovih modela, čak i kada je OpenAI to činio. To znači da je Pentagon mogao zaključiti da, budući da plaća Microsoftu – a ne izravno OpenAI-u – pravila OpenAI-a nisu relevantna. Legalno možda, ali etički? To je pitanje koje nitko ne želi postaviti. Posebno ne kada su u igri milijarde iz vojnog proračuna i tehnološka prednost nad konkurentima poput [Googlea](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-ml/google-cloud-and-the-department-of-defense-accelerate-ai-adoption) ili [Amazona](https://aws.amazon.com/government-education/government-defense/), koji također žure osigurati svoje vojne ugovore.

Najironičnije od svega je što OpenAI i dalje tvrdi kako je njegova tehnologija namijenjena „korisnicima diljem svijeta“, a ne „za štetu ljudima“. No, kada vaš najveći partner nudi vaše modele vojscima diljem svijeta – i to prije nego što ste službeno dopustili takvu upotrebu – te riječi zvuče prazno. Posebno kada se zna da su vojne primjene AI-a već dugo [realnost](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RR4327.html), od autonomnih dronova do algoritama za ciljanje. OpenAI-ova promjena politika u siječnju 2024. nije bila ništa drugo do *post facto* blagoslov za ono što se već događalo.

Ako postoji jedna lekcija iz ove priče, to je da u svijetu velikih tehnoloških korporacija i vojno-industrijskog kompleksa, *pravila vrijede dok god ih netko ne odluči ignorirati*. I kada su u pitanju milijarde, uvijek će se naći način.
