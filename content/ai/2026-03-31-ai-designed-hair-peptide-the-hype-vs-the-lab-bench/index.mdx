A team at [Kyungpook National University](https://en.knu.ac.kr) didn’t just stumble upon a hair growth peptide—they *computationally engineered* it. The novel MLPH peptide, born from algorithms rather than serendipity, is the latest test of whether AI can outpace traditional drug discovery in dermatology. Early data suggests it promotes hair growth without the hormonal side effects of finasteride or the scalp irritation of minoxidil, but here’s the catch: those claims come from *in vitro* and animal studies, not double-blind human trials.


The real story isn’t the peptide itself—it’s the method. By leveraging [molecular dynamics simulations](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-42102-4), the team sidestepped years of lab bench trial-and-error, a process that typically burns 80% of R&D budgets in dermatology. That’s a competitive edge, but it’s also a familiar pattern: computational biology delivers a flashy demo, while the path to FDA approval remains a slog of biology’s messy realities.


This isn’t the first AI-designed peptide to promise miracle growth—remember [OliX’s 2022 RNAi candidate](https://www.biospace.com/article/olix-pharmaceuticals-announces-positive-topline-results-from-phase-2a-study-of-olx101-for-androgenetic-alopecia/)?—but it might be the first to lean so hard on *safety* as its differentiator. Early signals suggest MLPH avoids the sexual dysfunction risks of DHT blockers, but without Phase III data, that’s still a calculated bet, not a guarantee.

<ArticleBreak />

The industry map here is straightforward: if MLPH’s safety profile holds, it pressures [Pfizer’s abandoned hair loss programs](https://www.fiercepharma.com/pharma/pfizer-drops-hair-loss-drug-after-lackluster-phase-2-data) and gives [Concert Pharmaceuticals’ CTP-543](https://www.concertpharma.com/pipeline/ctp-543/) a run for its money in the alopecia arena. But the bigger question is whether computational peptide design can scale beyond niche dermatology. Right now, the [GitHub activity](https://github.com/topics/peptide-design) around AI-driven protein engineering is heavy on academic tools and light on clinical translation—MLPH could change that, or it could join the graveyard of ‘promising’ pre-clinical assets.


Developer signals are cautiously optimistic. The [Rosetta Commons](https://www.rosettacommons.org/) community notes that while MLPH’s design pipeline isn’t open-source, its success validates their own tools for de novo peptide generation. Yet the reality gap looms: even with AI acceleration, peptide stability in human scalps is a notorious hurdle. Remember [Botox’s early peptide predecessors](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.281.5374.201)? Most failed not for lack of efficacy, but because they degraded before reaching target cells.


For all the noise about ‘next-generation’ therapeutics, the actual story is simpler: this is a bet on whether computational biology can finally turn the corner from *interesting* to *deployable*. The peptide’s safety claims are compelling, but the market’s seen this movie before—where the trailer (press release) outshines the film (clinical outcomes).
