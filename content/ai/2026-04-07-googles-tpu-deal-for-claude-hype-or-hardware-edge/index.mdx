Anthropic’s deal with Google and Broadcom to power [Claude](https://www.anthropic.com) on next-gen TPUs isn’t just another cloud partnership—it’s a bet on hardware that may not even exist yet. The [confirmed 2025 timeline](https://9to5google.com/2024/02/27/anthropic-google-tpu-deal/) for deployment means today’s announcement is more about securing capacity than shipping a product. Google’s TPUs have long been the underdog to NVIDIA’s GPUs, but this move suggests Anthropic is hedging against GPU shortages and Azure’s [growing AI infrastructure](https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/blog/azure-ai-infrastructure-updates/).


The real question isn’t whether Claude will run on TPUs—it’s whether those TPUs will outperform the [A100s and H100s](https://www.nvidia.com/en-us/data-center/products/) already dominating AI training. Google’s [TPU v5](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/compute/google-cloud-next-24-tpu-v5e-announcement) remains largely untested in production, and Broadcom’s involvement hints at custom silicon tweaks that could either accelerate performance or become another layer of proprietary lock-in.


Broadcom’s role is the sleeper detail here. The chipmaker isn’t just supplying hardware—it’s likely optimizing the stack for Anthropic’s workloads, a move that mirrors NVIDIA’s CUDA ecosystem play. If this works, Claude could see [real-world efficiency gains](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.11267); if it doesn’t, Anthropic’s just rented a very expensive testbed.

<ArticleBreak />

For all the talk of ‘next-gen’ hardware, the deal’s timing is the actual story. A 2025 deployment means Anthropic is planning for a world where today’s GPUs are obsolete—but also one where Google’s TPUs might still be playing catch-up. The [AI training market](https://www.semianalysis.com/p/google-tpu-v5e-deep-dive) is already bifurcating: NVIDIA for raw power, Google for cost-efficient scaling. This deal is Anthropic picking a side.


Developers aren’t waiting for press releases to react. On [GitHub](https://github.com) and [Hacker News](https://news.ycombinator.com), the consensus is cautious: TPUs *can* excel at inference, but training is another story. The lack of public benchmarks for TPU v5 means we’re flying blind on whether this is a strategic masterstroke or a [vendor lock-in gamble](https://www.theregister.com/2023/11/15/google_tpu_lock_in/).


The real signal here isn’t the partnership—it’s the silence on exclusivity. If Google’s TPUs become Claude’s primary backbone, Microsoft’s [Azure AI](https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/products/ai-services/) just lost a key customer. If not, this is just another cloud provider playing the ‘we’ve got the best chips’ marketing game.
