The [Runway AI Summit](https://www.wired.com/story/runway-ai-summit-2024/) last week played like a revival meeting for Hollywood’s AI faithful. Speakers framed generative tools as civilization-level shifts—**[fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_of_fire_by_early_humans)**, **[the printing press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press)**, the usual suspects—while glossing over the fact that most studios still treat AI as a glorified storyboarding assistant. The only discordant note came from *Star Wars* producer [Kathleen Kennedy](https://www.imdb.com/name/nm0448106/), who publicly questioned whether the tech was ready for prime time, let alone mythmaking.


That skepticism arrived at an awkward moment. The summit unfolded just days after the death of **[Sora](https://openai.com/sora)**, OpenAI’s high-profile text-to-video model, which had been positioned as the next leap forward before its abrupt demise. (Runway, notably, didn’t mention the elephant in the room.) The timing underscored a pattern: AI’s boosters rush to anoint each iteration as transformative, while its critics—like Kennedy—point to the [reality gap](https://techand.com/ai-reality-gap-2024) between flashy demos and what actually ships.


Early signals suggest the summit’s hype cycle followed a familiar script. Runway’s latest tools, per [leaked benchmarks](https://github.com/ai-benchmark/results-2024), still struggle with **consistent character rigging** and **lighting coherence**—problems that [VFX supervisors](https://www.vfxvoice.com/ai-in-vfx-2024/) say make them ‘useless for 90% of shots.’ Yet the marketing machine chugs on, repackaging incremental gains as paradigm shifts.

<ArticleBreak />

The real story isn’t the tech—it’s the **industry map**. Runway, backed by [Google Ventures](https://www.gv.com/) and [NVIDIA](https://www.nvidia.com/), is positioning itself as the ‘creative OS’ for studios, a play that pressures legacy VFX houses like [ILM](https://www.ilm.com/) and [Weta Digital](https://www.wetadigital.com/). Their pitch: *Why pay for artists when you can license our model?* But as Kennedy’s comments hint, the math doesn’t add up—yet. [GitHub activity](https://github.com/runwayml) around Runway’s tools remains flat, and indie devs [note](https://forums.unrealengine.com/t/runway-ai-integration-issues/567892) that ‘it’s faster to do it manually’ for complex scenes.


Meanwhile, the community’s irony meters are pegged. The summit’s closing tagline—**‘Thank You for Generating With Us!’**—became an instant [meme](https://twitter.com/hashtag/GeneratingWithUs) among VFX artists, who’ve spent years watching AI demos promise ‘one-click cinematic quality.’ The [Blender](https://www.blender.org/) and [Unreal Engine](https://www.unrealengine.com/) forums are filled with the same refrain: *Show us the pipeline, not the sizzle reel.*


If there’s a shift here, it’s not in the tech—it’s in the **marketing’s growing desperation**. After Sora’s collapse, the industry’s AI acolytes are leaning harder on metaphor (‘fire! printing press!’) and softer on [deployment data](https://techand.com/ai-deployment-tracker). That’s not how revolutions start. That’s how bubbles inflate.
