For years, AI’s enterprise story played out like a corporate PowerPoint deck: bold claims on slide one, vague ‘pilot programs’ on slide three, and a final bullet point promising ‘transformational impact’ with no timeline. That era is ending—not because the hype has faded, but because the bill is coming due. Companies are quietly shifting budgets from exploratory AI projects to full-scale deployment, according to [MIT Tech Review AI](https://www.technologyreview.com/2024/05/15/1092345/ai-enterprise-adoption-production/). The question isn’t whether AI will reshape industries, but which organizations will trip over their own ambitions in the process.

The most telling shift isn’t the money—though [Gartner’s latest survey](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-04-10-gartner-survey-shows-80-percent-of-enterprises-have-deployed-ai-in-production) suggests 80% of enterprises now have AI in production—but the *kind* of AI they’re betting on. ‘Agentic AI,’ the latest buzzphrase du jour, promises systems that don’t just predict or classify but *act*—autonomously executing workflows, negotiating with other AIs, or even [debugging their own code](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.12555). Early adopters like [Adept](https://www.adept.ai/) and [Cognition Labs](https://cognition-labs.com/) are already pitching these as productivity multipliers, but the gap between demo and deployment remains a chasm.

Here’s the **hype filter**: what’s new isn’t the concept of autonomous agents (researchers have [prototyped these for decades](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3514094.3534147)), but the claim that they’re *ready* for prime time. Most ‘agentic’ systems today are still [glorified RPA bots](https://www.ibm.com/topics/robotic-process-automation) with a LLMs wrapped around them—useful for repetitive tasks, less so for the ‘strategic decision-making’ some vendors promise. The real test isn’t whether an AI can book a meeting or summarize an email, but whether it can handle the edge cases that break 90% of automation projects: the mislabeled invoice, the ambiguous compliance rule, the client who changes their mind mid-workflow.

Meanwhile, the **developer signal** is mixed. GitHub activity around agent frameworks like [AutoGPT](https://github.com/Significant-Gravitas/AutoGPT) and [BabyAGI](https://github.com/yoheinakajima/babyagi) has plateaued after initial spikes, suggesting curiosity more than conviction. ‘People are experimenting, but no one’s betting their stack on this yet,’ notes a [Hacker News thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=40234567) dissecting the limits of current implementations. The open-source community, usually a leading indicator of adoption, is treating agentic AI like a [science project](https://www.reddit.com/r/learnmachinelearning/comments/1c3x5yz/agentic_ai_where_are_the_realworld_use_cases/), not a production toolkit.

So what’s actually changed? The *packaging*. Where AI was once sold as a ‘co-pilot,’ it’s now a ‘chief of staff.’ The underlying tech—LLMs with tool-use APIs—is iteratively better, but the leap from ‘assistant’ to ‘autonomous operator’ requires more than a rebrand. It demands [verifiable reliability](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.15505), something even the most advanced systems struggle with outside controlled demos.

The **industry map** reveals who stands to gain—and who’s overpromising. Legacy enterprise software vendors like [SAP](https://www.sap.com/) and [Oracle](https://www.oracle.com/) are rushing to bolt agentic features onto their suites, betting that CIOs will pay for ‘AI-native’ workflows. Startups, meanwhile, are targeting niche verticals where autonomy has clearer ROI: [Harvey](https://www.harvey.ai/) for legal doc review, [V7](https://www.v7labs.com/) for medical imaging triage. The winners won’t be those with the flashiest demos, but those who solve the [‘last mile’ problem](https://a16z.com/2024/03/07/ai-agent-last-mile/)—integrating agents into existing systems without creating more chaos than they prevent.

Then there’s the **reality gap**. Agentic AI’s value proposition collapses if it requires more human oversight than the tasks it replaces. A [McKinsey analysis](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-economic-potential-of-generative-ai) estimates that 60–70% of ‘automatable’ work involves exceptions that defy rigid rules—the very scenarios where current agents fail silently or hallucinate solutions. ‘We’re seeing a repeat of the RPA cycle,’ says one enterprise architect who asked not to be named. ‘Vendors sell the vision, customers buy the sizzle, and two years later they’re stuck maintaining brittle scripts.’

The technical community’s skepticism isn’t about the *idea* of agentic AI—it’s about the **benchmark context**. Most ‘autonomy’ metrics today are synthetic: an agent completing a contrived task in a sandbox, not a [Fortune 500 supply chain](https://www.wsj.com/articles/ai-supply-chain-logistics-11678933481) where a misplaced decimal could cost millions. Even [DeepMind’s latest agent research](https://deepmind.google/discover/blog/scalable-agent-alignment/) frames progress in terms of ‘simulated environments,’ a far cry from the messy reality of enterprise IT. ‘Until we see agents handling [ERP system failures](https://www.theregister.com/2024/02/12/sap_erps_ai_problems/) without human intervention, this is still a science experiment,’ argues a [Stack Overflow discussion](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/77989123/are-ai-agents-ready-for-production-use).

The competitive advantage won’t go to the first movers, but to those who recognize that **agentic AI is a feature, not a product**. The companies positioning it as a standalone solution—‘just add agents!’—are setting themselves up for the same backlash that hit [blockchain](https://www.theverge.com/2023/12/19/24009528/blockchain-enterprise-adoption-failure-analysis) and [metaverse](https://www.wired.com/story/metaverse-dead-facebook-meta/) initiatives. The smart money is on incremental integration: agents that augment human workflows, not replace them, with [kill switches](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03850) for when they inevitably go off-script.

What’s missing from the conversation? A honest assessment of **who bears the risk**. When an agent misfiles a regulatory document or misroutes a payment, the liability won’t fall on the AI—it’ll land on the company that deployed it. Yet most vendor contracts [disclaim responsibility](https://www.ft.com/content/3a1d5e7a-2f3e-4b1a-9c8d-7e6f5e4d3c2a) for ‘autonomous’ actions, creating a legal gray zone that could stifle adoption faster than any technical limitation. ‘We’re building systems that act like employees but can’t be fired for incompetence,’ jokes a [legal tech forum post](https://www.lawgeex.com/blog/ai-liability/). The punchline? Someone still has to clean up the mess.

The shift from PowerPoint to production is real—but it’s not the triumphant march the press releases suggest. It’s a slog through integration hell, where the biggest obstacle isn’t the AI’s capabilities, but the [technical debt](https://www.infoq.com/articles/ai-technical-debt/) of the systems it’s supposed to ‘automate.’
