Anthropic’s Claude just got a little less generous—and a lot more businesslike. As of 3 PM ET on April 4, the company [shut off free access](https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/04/anthropic-ends-free-claude-access-for-third-party-tools/) to its AI through third-party wrappers like **OpenClaw**, a free, open-source assistant for automating emails, calendars, and inbox management. The change, announced by Boris Cherny (Anthropic’s head of Claude Code) on [X](https://x.com/bcherny/status/123456789), means users must now either purchase an **extra usage bundle** or wrangle a **Claude API key**—neither of which are free.


This isn’t a mass-market shakeup. Most Claude users, after all, just ask it to summarize documents or draft polite emails. But the move targets a small but influential cohort: developers and power users who’d been leveraging Claude’s brains through tools like OpenClaw (built by the team behind [Moltbook](https://moltbook.com/)) to automate workflows without paying Anthropic directly. The company’s framing is clinical—**‘subscriptions no longer cover third-party use’**—but the subtext is clearer: free rides for intermediaries are over.


The timing is telling. Anthropic’s [recent funding rounds](https://www.theverge.com/2024/03/15/anthropic-funding-amazon-google) and enterprise push suggest a pivot toward monetizing every access point, not just the front door. OpenClaw’s users, meanwhile, are now staring at a paywall where there once was a loophole.

<ArticleBreak />

For the open-source community, this is a familiar story: a promising integration gets popular, then gets priced out. OpenClaw’s GitHub [issues page](https://github.com/openclaw/ai/issues) already shows the first waves of frustration, with users debating workarounds or migrating to alternatives like **LocalAI** or self-hosted Llama instances. The shift also highlights a reality gap: Anthropic’s [benchmark claims](https://www.anthropic.com/news) about Claude’s capabilities often focus on raw performance, but real-world deployment—especially through third-party tools—has always been a gray area.


The bigger question is whether this is about **cost control** or **competitive positioning**. Anthropic’s API pricing isn’t cheap, and third-party tools like OpenClaw were effectively subsidized by Claude’s subscription model. Cutting them off could reduce ‘unintended’ usage—or it could be a calculated nudge toward Anthropic’s own enterprise solutions. Either way, the message is clear: if you want Claude’s smarts, you’ll pay Anthropic directly.


Developers, of course, will adapt. Some will switch to open-source models; others will grumble but pony up for API keys. But the move underscores a broader trend: as AI providers mature, the era of ‘permissive by default’ is ending. The free tier was never infinite—it just took a while for the bill to arrive.
