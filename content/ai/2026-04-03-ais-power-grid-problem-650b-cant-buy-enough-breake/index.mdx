Cloud providers are discovering an inconvenient truth: you can’t train a large language model on a prayer and a power strip. With [half of planned US data center builds delayed or canceled](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/cloud-giants-plan-to-spend-650-billion-on-ai-infrastructure-this-year-but-power-shortages-are-a-major-obstacle), the AI gold rush is colliding with the prosaic limits of electrical grids and supply chains. The $650 billion earmarked for AI infrastructure this year—enough to build [130 nuclear reactors](https://www.eia.gov/tools/faqs/faq.php?id=427&t=3) at current costs—can’t overcome the fact that transformers, switchgear, and high-voltage cables now have lead times stretching into 2025.


The bottleneck isn’t GPUs or even cooling systems; it’s the [‘long-lead’ power components](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/power-crisis-data-center-industry-faces-up-to-two-year-wait-for-key-electrical-gear/) that turn electricity into usable current for servers. These aren’t sexy obstacles—no one’s tweeting about ‘breaker shortages’—but they’re the difference between a [demo running on a pre-cooled rack](https://www.nextplatform.com/2024/03/12/the-hidden-costs-of-ai-power-and-cooling/) and a production cluster that can handle 24/7 inference loads. Meanwhile, China’s export controls on critical electrical parts add another layer of friction, turning what should be a logistics problem into a geopolitical one.


This isn’t just a capacity issue. It’s a **reality gap** between the ‘AI is eating the world’ narrative and the fact that, for now, AI is mostly eating through power purchase agreements. The [International Energy Agency warns](https://www.iea.org/reports/electricity-2024) that data center electricity demand could double by 2026—yet grid upgrades move at the speed of regulatory approval, not VC funding rounds.

<ArticleBreak />

The industry map here is brutal for latecomers. Companies that locked in power contracts years ago—think [Microsoft’s 2021 deal for 960MW in Virginia](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/microsoft-secures-960mw-of-capacity-in-virginia/)—are now sitting pretty, while newcomers scramble for scraps. This isn’t about who has the best model; it’s about who has the best lawyers to navigate [interconnection queues](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/data-center-power-demand-grid-interconnection/705120/) that can stretch for years. Even hyperscalers aren’t immune: [Google’s Tennessee data center](https://www.tennessean.com/story/money/2024/02/15/google-data-center-clarksville-tennessee-power-demand/72589203007/) faced delays when local utilities balked at the load.


Developer signals are mixed but telling. On GitHub, there’s a quiet surge in projects optimizing for [‘power-constrained training’](https://github.com/topics/power-efficient-ml), but the real energy is in workarounds: more [quantization tricks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.08328), aggressive batching, and even [‘dark silicon’ approaches](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3578360) that deliberately idle parts of chips to stay under power caps. The open-source community isn’t waiting for the grid to catch up—it’s coding as if the grid never will.


For all the talk of AI’s exponential growth, the actual curve looks more like a [step function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Step_function): flat until the power comes online, then a vertical spike. That’s not how VC pitches describe it, but it’s how engineers are planning for it.
