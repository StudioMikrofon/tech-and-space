BRINC’s new [Guardian drone](https://arstechnica.com/cars/2024/02/brincs-new-police-drone-uses-starlink-carries-narcan-chases-vehicles-at-60mph/) packs a spec sheet that reads like a sci-fi wishlist: Starlink connectivity, Narcan delivery, and a top speed of 60mph—all while the company calls it the [“most capable 911 response drone ever”](https://www.brincdrones.com/guardian). The hook is obvious: a drone that can outrun fleeing vehicles, beam live footage via satellite, and even administer overdose reversal medication mid-flight. For police departments drowning in pursuit risks and response delays, this looks like a godsend.

But the real question isn’t what it *can* do in a controlled demo—it’s what it *will* do after the press release fades. The Guardian’s headline capabilities rely on a stack of assumptions: uninterrupted Starlink coverage (good luck in urban canyons or remote valleys), a payload system that won’t misfire Narcan into a bystander’s windshield, and a battery that lasts longer than the chase scene in the promo video. BRINC isn’t wrong to highlight these features, but the distance between a [YouTube-ready sprint](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=example) and a shift-ready tool is measured in red tape, not miles per hour.

Early adopters will face the usual trifecta of robotics deployment friction: FAA waivers that take months, departments reluctant to trust a drone over a cruiser, and the unsexy reality that most police chases don’t happen on empty Nevada highways. The Guardian’s 60mph claim is technically impressive—until you remember that [real-world drone operations](https://www.faa.gov/uas) rarely exceed 40mph, and that’s before accounting for wind, obstacles, or the small matter of not crashing into power lines.

<ArticleBreak />

Let’s talk about the Narcan. A drone that can deliver overdose reversal drugs sounds like a lifesaver—literally—until you map the use case. Overdoses don’t happen in neat, GPS-pinned locations with clear landing zones. They happen in alleys, under bridges, and inside buildings where a drone’s payload drop would be lucky to hit the right sidewalk. BRINC’s marketing leans hard on the [“save lives” angle](https://www.brincdrones.com/guardian#medical), but the logistics of precision aerial drug delivery in chaotic environments remain… optimistic. Even if the tech works flawlessly, the liability questions alone—what if the dose lands in the wrong hands?—could ground the feature before it takes off.

Then there’s Starlink. Satellite connectivity is a legitimate breakthrough for beyond-line-of-sight operations, but it’s not magic. Latency, obstructions, and the fact that [Starlink’s mobile service](https://www.starlink.com/mobile) isn’t designed for high-speed drone relays mean the Guardian’s “always-on” promise has asterisks. Field tests in dense cities or mountainous terrain will reveal whether this is a robust solution or just another demo-friendly gimmick.

The Guardian’s real test won’t be its top speed or payload capacity—it’ll be whether BRINC can navigate the bureaucratic and technical minefield that turns prototypes into standard-issue gear. Right now, this is a drone that *could* change emergency response, if the stars align: regulators play ball, departments trust the tech, and the hardware survives the jump from scripted demos to unscripted reality. That’s a lot of ifs for a machine that’s already being sold as a finished product.
