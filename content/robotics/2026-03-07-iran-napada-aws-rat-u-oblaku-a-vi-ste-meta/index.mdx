part1_en: "When Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) [claimed responsibility](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/iran-says-it-targeted-aws-data-center-in-bahrain-hosting-us-military-workloads) for a cyberattack on an AWS data center in Bahrain, it wasn’t just another skirmish in the endless cat-and-mouse of state-sponsored hacking. It was a deliberate strike against the cloud infrastructure underpinning U.S. military operations—a reminder that modern warfare doesn’t just unfold on battlefields or in the electromagnetic spectrum, but in the racks of hyperscale data centers hosting classified workloads. The target wasn’t a server farm in the abstract; it was a node in the [Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability (JWCC)](https://www.defense.gov/News/Releases/Release/Article/3236820/dod-awards-joint-warfighting-cloud-capability-jwcc-contract/), the Pentagon’s $9 billion multi-vendor cloud initiative designed to push AI, logistics, and command-and-control systems to the tactical edge.

This wasn’t a theoretical vulnerability exploit. The IRGC’s statement explicitly tied the attack to AWS’s role in hosting U.S. military data, framing it as retaliation for perceived aggression. While details of the attack’s success remain [unverified](https://www.reuters.com/technology/iran-says-it-targeted-aws-data-center-bahrain-hosting-us-military-workloads-2024-01-15/), the admission alone forces a reckoning: cloud providers are no longer neutral utilities but critical infrastructure with geopolitical bullseyes. The question isn’t whether AWS or Microsoft Azure can withstand distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks—it’s whether their global footprint, optimized for latency and compliance, is now a liability in an era where data centers are treated as legitimate military targets.

The implications ripple beyond cybersecurity. AWS’s Bahrain region, [launched in 2019](https://aws.amazon.com/about-aws/global-infrastructure/regions_az/), was marketed as a bridge between the Middle East and global cloud services, with three Availability Zones and compliance certifications tailored to regional governments. But geopolitical tensions don’t respect compliance frameworks. When a cloud region becomes a proxy for state conflict, the assumptions underpinning multi-cloud resilience—redundancy, failover, geographic distribution—start to fray. If a nation-state can justify targeting a commercial data center because it hosts military workloads, what does that mean for the thousands of civilian customers sharing the same physical infrastructure? The line between ‘dual-use’ and ‘collateral damage’ just got blurrier."
part2_en: "The attack also exposes a gap in how cloud providers communicate risk to enterprise and government customers. AWS’s [Shared Responsibility Model](https://aws.amazon.com/compliance/shared-responsibility-model/) clearly delineates security obligations, but it doesn’t account for kinetic or hybrid threats where the data center itself becomes a battleground. Traditional cybersecurity frameworks—zero trust, encryption, access controls—assume the hardware remains intact. When the threat is a missile strike or a sabotage team breaching a facility, those frameworks are insufficient. The Pentagon’s JWCC contract includes clauses for ‘disaster recovery’ and ‘contingency operations,’ but it’s unclear whether ‘disaster’ now includes state-sponsored physical attacks on cloud regions hosting military data.

For the robotics and autonomous systems reliant on cloud backends—drones processing ISR data, logistics bots coordinating supply chains, or AI models training on edge-collected intelligence—this attack is a wake-up call. The assumption that cloud services are ‘always on’ underpins everything from swarm coordination to real-time target recognition. If a data center in Bahrain can be disrupted by geopolitical tensions, what happens to the [autonomous resupply drones](https://www.defense.gov/News/News-Stories/Article/Article/3360020/pentagon-tests-autonomous-resupply-drones-in-pacific/) the U.S. military is testing in the Pacific, or the AI-driven [predictive maintenance systems](https://www.ibm.com/blogs/think/ai-predictive-maintenance/) keeping F-35s airborne? The cloud isn’t just a backend; it’s a single point of failure with a physical address.

The broader issue is that cloud providers have spent a decade optimizing for scalability and cost efficiency, not geopolitical resilience. AWS’s Bahrain region, like its [Israel (Tel Aviv) region](https://aws.amazon.com/about-aws/global-infrastructure/regions_az/#Israel), was designed to meet local data sovereignty laws and reduce latency for regional customers. But sovereignty cuts both ways: a data center in a contested region isn’t just a node in a global network—it’s a potential casus belli. The industry has no playbook for this. Do cloud providers now need to treat facilities in geopolitical hotspots as ‘hardened’ assets, with the same physical defenses as embassies? Or will governments demand that military workloads be siloed in sovereign clouds, fragmenting the very multi-cloud flexibility the Pentagon sought with JWCC?

What’s missing from the conversation is a clear-eyed assessment of how cloud infrastructure fits into modern hybrid warfare. The IRGC’s attack wasn’t about stealing data or disrupting services—it was about signaling that cloud regions are fair game. For the robotics and autonomous systems community, which increasingly relies on cloud-based simulation, training, and command-and-control, the message is clear: resilience can’t stop at the software layer. The next generation of military robots might need to assume their cloud backends are compromised—or worse, destroyed."

Kada je Iranova Revolucionarna garda odlučila napasti tri AWS-ova podatkovna centra u UAE-u i Bahreinu, nije se radilo o klasičnom ratnom sukobu. Cilj nije bio niti naftna postrojenja niti vojne baze, već serveri na kojima, prema Teheranu, leže američki vojni podaci. Da, čitate dobro: umjesto tenkova, meta su postala računala koja pokreću vaše omiljene aplikacije, streamanje serija ili – ako ste nesretni – i vašu tvrtkinu infrastrukturu. Ovo nije prvi put da se cloud pretvara u bojište, ali je rijetko tko to otvoreno priznao. Tom’s Hardware donosi detalje kako je IRGC eksplicitno potvrdio napade, navodeći kako je AWS-ov centar u Bahreinu izabran jer tamo 'hosta vojne radne opterećenja SAD-a'. Što, naravno, postavlja pitanje: koliko je vaš business-critical SaaS zapravo collateral damage u nečijem geopolitičkom obračunu? AWS, naravno, šuti kao grob – standardna praksa kada korporacije postanu nehotice sudionici rata. Problem je u tome što su cloud provajderi već godinama prodavali sigurnost kao svoju glavnu prednost. 'Multi-region redundancy' zvuči impresivno dok ne shvatite da vaši podaci mogu završiti na serveru koji je – iz političkih razloga – postao legitimna meta. Iran ovdje nije ništa novo izumio; samo je pokazao kako se infrastruktura kao usluga (IaaS) može pretvoriti u infrastrukturu kao metu. A ako mislite da je ovo problem samo za tvrtke s vojnim ugovorima, pogrešite. Kada dronovi gađaju podatkovne centre, ne pitaju tko je tamo smjestio svoju e-trgovinu ili HR bazu podataka. Da stvari budu gore, AWS i slični divovi dugo ignoriraju pozive za transparenciju o tome tko sve koristi njihove resurse. 'Ne znamo tko je naš susjed na serveru' nije samo šala iz shared hosting era – to je sada sigurnosni rizik. Ako vam se čini da je ovo preuveličavanje, sjetite se incidenta s Azure-om 2022., kada je pad usluge oborio sve od bankarstva do zdravstvenih sustava. Razlika? Tada je kriv bio human error; sada imamo namjerno gađanje. Ako ste ikada razmišljali o digitalnom suverenitetu, evo ga u najgorem mogućem obliku: vaši podaci kao strateški resurs u tuđem ratu. I ne, rješenje nije 'prelazak na lokalne servere' – jer tko garantira da vaš Hetzner ili OVH neće postati sljedeća meta? Pitanje nije je li cloud siguran, već čija sigurnost je prioritet kada dođe do sukoba.

Iranovi napadi na AWS nisu samo tehnički incident, već simptom mnogo dubokog problema: cloud infrastruktura je postala kritična, ali je njena regulacija ostala u 20. stoljeću. Dok se tvrtke natječu tko će ponuditi više zona dostupnosti ili brže procesore, pitanje tko kontrolira te zone i ko ih može iskoristiti ostaje neodgovoreno. EU je pokušala s GAIA-X projektom, ali dok god postoje provajderi koji mogu odbiti audite zbog 'poslovne tajne', sve su to prazne riječi. Zanimljivo je kako se o ovome malo priča u tech medijima. Umjesto analize rizika, dobivamo članke o tome kako AWS štedi energiju ili koliko je brzo njihovo novo GPU ureganje. Kao da je efikasnost važnija od činjenice da vaš backup može završiti na serveru koji je de facto vojna baza. A kad kažemo 'vojna baza', ne mislimo na klasične barake – mislimo na JEDI ugovor vrijedan 10 milijardi dolara, gdje se Pentagon oslanja na istu infrastrukturu koju koriste startupi i NGO-i. Ovdje valja istaknuti i hipokriziju zapadnih vlada. S jedne strane, SAD kažu kako je kinesko prisutnost u cloud-u 'prijetnja nacionalnoj sigurnosti'. S druge, isti ti cloudovi hostaju njihove vojne sustave – ali to, čini se, nije problem dok god je their cloud. Iran je samo pokazao kako se ta dvostruka mjerila mogu okrenuti protiv njih. I ne radi se samo o SAD-u: izvješća pokazuju kako europski podaci često završavaju na američkim serverima zbog 'efikasnosti', čime EU gubi kontrolu nad vlastitim informacijama. Što onda raditi? Prvo, prestati vjerovati da je 'public cloud' neutralan prostor. Drugo, zahtijevati od provajdera transparenciju o tome tko dijeli infrastrukturu s vama – barem na razini regije. Treće, razmisliti o multicloud strategiji koja nije samo marketing već geopolitička diverzifikacija. Jer ako ste naučili išta iz ovog incidenta, to je da niti jedan oblak nije otporan na dronove – ni fizički, ni metaforički. Na kraju, pitanje nije samo 'jesmo li sigurni?', već '**čija sigurnost se štiti na naš trošak?**'
