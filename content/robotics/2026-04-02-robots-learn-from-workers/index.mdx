Czech startup RoboTwin has developed technology that allows factory workers to teach robots new skills by demonstrating the task in person. This approach eliminates the need for complex coding, making it easier for workers to train robots for dirty or dangerous factory floor tasks. According to [Robohub](https://robohub.org/), the system enables workers to perform the job once and RoboTwin’s technology turns those demonstrations into actionable robot instructions. 




The technology has the potential to disrupt traditional robotics training methods, which often require specialized robotics engineers for task programming. However, the exact mechanism behind RoboTwin’s demonstration-to-code conversion is not described, leaving some uncertainty about the system's capabilities. As reported by [The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/), the use of robotics in manufacturing is becoming increasingly popular, with many companies looking to automate dirty or dangerous tasks.

<ArticleBreak />

While the technology shows promise, there are still several challenges to overcome before it can be widely adopted. One of the main limitations is the need for a controlled environment, which may not always be possible in real-world factory settings. Additionally, the system's ability to scale up to more complex tasks and larger deployments is still uncertain. As noted by [Wired](https://www.wired.com/), the use of robotics in manufacturing is not without its challenges, including the need for significant investment in hardware and software. 




Despite these challenges, the potential benefits of RoboTwin's technology are significant. By allowing factory workers to train robots more easily, the system could help to increase productivity and reduce the risk of injury. As [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/) reports, the use of robotics in manufacturing is expected to continue to grow in the coming years, with many companies looking to automate tasks and improve efficiency.

The hype surrounding robotics demos often overlooks the significant challenges of real-world deployment. In reality, the journey from lab to factory floor is often long and arduous. The tech demo culture can create unrealistic expectations about the capabilities of robotics systems.
