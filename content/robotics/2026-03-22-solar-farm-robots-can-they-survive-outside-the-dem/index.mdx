The US solar industry needs to move faster—[1.4 GW of new capacity](https://www.eia.gov/electricity/monthly/epm_table_grapher.php?t=epmt_6_07_b) must come online *monthly* to meet 2035 decarbonization targets. Enter **Terafab V2**, Berkeley-based [Terabase Energy](https://terabase.energy/)’s automated solar farm constructor, now exiting field tests and shipping commercially. The pitch is straightforward: autonomous robots and AI replace slow, labor-intensive panel mounting, promising **2x faster deployment** in ideal conditions.

But here’s the catch: *ideal conditions* rarely exist. The [demo videos](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=example) show Terafab’s articulated arms placing panels with millimeter precision on flat, obstacle-free terrain. Real solar farms? Think **uneven grading, wind gusts, and mud after rain**—the kind of variables that turn ‘autonomous’ into ‘remote-supervised’ faster than a marketing deck can say *scalable*.

Terabase isn’t wrong about the need. Solar installations [account for 53% of new US electric capacity](https://www.seia.org/solar-industry-research-data) in 2024, but labor shortages and permitting delays strangle growth. A robot that *actually* works could unclog the bottleneck. The question isn’t whether Terafab V2 is impressive—it’s whether it’s **deployment-ready** or just another polished demo waiting for reality to intrude.

<ArticleBreak />

Let’s talk hardware. Terafab V2’s specs—[payload capacity](https://terabase.energy/terafab), battery life, and terrain tolerance—aren’t public, but industry chatter suggests **payload limits under 100 kg per arm** and **operational windows constrained by weather**. That’s fine for lightweight panels but problematic for **racking systems or heavy-duty mounts** required in high-wind zones. Then there’s maintenance: autonomous or not, [dust accumulation on sensors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S095741742030456X) and mechanical wear in 24/7 operation demand **human oversight**—the kind Terabase’s marketing glosses over.

The real test isn’t whether Terafab V2 *can* build a solar farm—it’s whether it can do so **cheaper and more reliably than humans** at scale. Early adopters like [NextEra Energy](https://www.nexteraenergy.com/) are watching, but mass deployment hinges on **three unsexy factors**: certification for varied terrains, proof of long-term reliability, and a total cost of ownership that beats manual labor. Right now, Terabase is selling a **promise of automation**, not a turnkey solution.

And then there’s the elephant in the room: **most solar delays aren’t construction**. Permitting, grid interconnection, and supply chain snags dwarf the time saved by faster panel placement. Terafab V2 might shave weeks off a 6-month project—but if the grid connection is backlogged for a year, does it matter?

Demo videos love to show robots working flawlessly in controlled environments. The unspoken rule of robotics? The first time it encounters a rock it wasn’t programmed to avoid, the ‘autonomous’ label starts looking like a flexible term.
