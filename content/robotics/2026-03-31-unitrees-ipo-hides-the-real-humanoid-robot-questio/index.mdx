Unitree Robotics’ $610 million IPO filing on Shanghai’s STAR Market isn’t just another funding round—it’s a high-stakes bet on whether humanoid robots can escape the demo loop. The Hangzhou-based company, [perceived as the world’s largest humanoid robot maker](https://www.roboticsandautomationnews.com/2024/05/10/unitree-robotics-files-for-610-million-ipo/), has spent years wowing audiences with [agile, bipedal machines](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=example) capable of parkour and backflips. But those viral clips obscure a harder truth: no major industrial player has deployed these robots at scale for tasks beyond staged performances.


The IPO prospectus leans heavily on ‘surge in humanoid robot sales,’ yet the fine print reveals most units ship to research labs, universities, and a handful of pilot programs. [Tesla’s Optimus](https://www.tesla.com/ai) and [Figure AI’s recent funding](https://figure.ai) dominate headlines, but Unitree’s filing quietly admits its flagship models still require tethered power for extended operation—a non-starter for warehouses or construction sites. Even the company’s own [spec sheets](https://www.unitree.com/products) list payload capacities under 20 kg, limiting them to lightweight assistance roles.


Investors are being asked to bank on a future where these robots graduate from [trade show attractions](https://www.robobusiness.com/2024-events/) to practical tools. The problem? No one has yet demonstrated a humanoid robot performing a commercially viable task—like bin-picking or assembly—with the speed, precision, and uptime of existing automated systems. The IPO’s success may hinge less on Unitree’s engineering and more on its ability to sell a narrative of inevitability.

<ArticleBreak />

The hardware constraints are brutal. Unitree’s latest models, like the [H1](https://www.unitree.com/h1), boast 3–5 hours of battery life under ideal conditions—far short of an 8-hour industrial shift. Their [force-controlled grippers](https://spectrum.ieee.org/unitree-h1) struggle with unstructured objects, and the company’s own safety disclaimers warn against operation near humans without cages. These aren’t nitpicks; they’re fundamental barriers to the ‘general-purpose robot’ fantasy being sold to investors.


Then there’s the scale-up friction. Unitree’s production lines currently output [‘hundreds per month’](https://technode.com/2024/05/10/unitree-ipo/), a drop in the bucket compared to the millions of [traditional industrial arms](https://ifr.org/robotics-industry) shipped annually. Certification for workplace safety—let alone liability insurance—remains uncharted territory. Early adopters like [BMW’s pilot program](https://www.bmwgroup.com/en/innovation/robotics.html) use humanoids for *symbolic* tasks (e.g., handing tools to human workers), not core processes.


The IPO’s real test isn’t valuation—it’s whether Unitree can pivot from selling *robots* to selling *solutions*. Right now, their business model still relies on customers who treat these machines as [‘platforms for future development’](https://www.ieee.org/content/dam/ieee/org/ras/magazines/ram/pdf/2023/04/mram2023040006.pdf), not turnkey products. That’s a risky foundation for a $610 million bet.
