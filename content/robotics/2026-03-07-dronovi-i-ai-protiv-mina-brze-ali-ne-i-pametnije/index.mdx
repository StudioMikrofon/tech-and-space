Landmines don’t care about technological progress. In 2024, they killed 1,945 people and maimed 4,325 more—[90% of them civilians, nearly half children](https://www.the-monitor.org/en-gb/reports/2024/landmine-monitor-2024/casualties-and-victim-assistance.aspx), according to the Landmine Monitor. Meanwhile, demining teams painstakingly removed 105,640 mines the same year, a number that sounds impressive until you compare it to the estimated [110 million mines still buried worldwide](https://www.icrc.org/en/document/anti-personnel-mines-facts-and-figures). Enter the latest wave of drone-and-AI demining systems, pitched as faster, cheaper, and safer than human crews. But speed in a controlled demo isn’t the same as reliability in a monsoon-soaked field in Cambodia or a war-torn stretch of Ukraine.

The core promise is automation: drones like the [UK’s Mine Kafon Drone](https://www.minekafon.org/drone) or [Swiss Foundation’s Dronut](https://www.fsd.ch/en/innovation/dronut/) map minefields with LiDAR and multispectral cameras, while AI algorithms flag suspicious objects for human verification. Some systems, like [Norway’s FFU demining drone](https://www.ffu.no/en/projects/demining-drone), even claim to detonate mines with precision explosives. The marketing language is sleek—‘autonomous,’ ‘scalable,’ ‘force multiplier’—but the fine print reveals a different story. Most of these systems are still in pilot phases, deployed in small, controlled zones where mine types and terrain are pre-mapped. The [HALO Trust](https://www.halotrust.org/), one of the world’s largest demining NGOs, has tested drones in Angola and Somalia but notes that ‘AI-assisted’ still means humans do 80% of the work. ‘It’s a tool, not a replacement,’ says a HALO field operator. ‘The drone spots anomalies. We still dig.’

The gap between demo and deployment becomes clearer when you look at the hardware. Most demining drones have a [maximum payload of 5–10 kg](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S221067072100627X), limiting them to lightweight sensors or small explosive charges. Battery life typically caps at 30–45 minutes—enough for a few hectares if the wind cooperates. Then there’s the sensing problem: [metal-detecting drones struggle with plastic mines](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-66893-2), while ground-penetrating radar (GPR) gets confused by roots, rocks, or even [uneven soil moisture](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9042106). ‘In a lab, you can train AI on pristine datasets,’ says Dr. Elena Petrovska, a robotics researcher at TU Delft. ‘In the field, you’re dealing with data noise, occlusions, and mines that have been buried for decades—corroded, shifted, or half-buried in vegetation.’

The use-case reality is even messier. Demining isn’t a single problem; it’s a spectrum. In post-conflict zones like Colombia or Iraq, mines are often scattered in rural areas with poor GPS coverage and no infrastructure for drone recharging. In Ukraine, Russian forces have [deployed ‘smart’ mines with anti-tampering sensors](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/mar/15/ukraine-russia-landmines-booby-traps-civilians), which can detect and resist drone-based detection. ‘We’ve seen mines rigged to explode when disturbed by a magnetic signature,’ says a Ukrainian deminer who requested anonymity. ‘A drone flying overhead might as well be a dinner bell.’

Then there’s the scale-up friction. Certification is a nightmare: most countries require demining tech to meet [IMAS standards](https://www.mineactionstandards.org/), which mandate 99.6% clearance accuracy—a threshold no AI system has reliably hit in real-world tests. Cost is another barrier. A single [Mine Kafon Drone system](https://www.minekafon.org/drone) runs about $250,000, plus training and maintenance. For comparison, a trained demining dog costs $10,000 and can work for years. ‘The economics only make sense if you’re clearing thousands of hectares,’ says a logistics officer with the [UN Mine Action Service](https://mineaction.org/). ‘For most NGOs, it’s cheaper to stick with metal detectors and probes.’

The biggest unanswered question isn’t whether drones *can* help—it’s whether they can help *where it matters most*. The majority of mine casualties occur in [low-income countries](https://www.icrc.org/en/document/landmines-explosive-remnants-war-2023) with limited technical infrastructure. A system that requires high-res satellite uplinks, spare lithium batteries, and on-site AI specialists isn’t just impractical; it’s irrelevant. ‘The tech is solving for the wrong constraints,’ argues Petrovska. ‘The bottleneck isn’t detection speed. It’s access, safety, and the fact that 70% of minefields are in areas with active conflict.’ Until drones can operate in GPS-denied zones, resist jamming, and distinguish a mine from a soda can with 100% certainty, they’ll remain what they are: expensive adjuncts to the real work of demining—done by humans, one careful step at a time.
