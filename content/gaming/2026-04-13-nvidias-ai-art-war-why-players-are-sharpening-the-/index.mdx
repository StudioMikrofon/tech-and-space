Dave Oshry isn’t mincing words. The CEO of indie studio New Blood Interactive has [called for a full-blown boycott](https://www.rockpapershotgun.com) of Nvidia’s DLSS 5, framing the tech’s AI-driven rendering as a threat to game art itself. His argument? That Nvidia’s neural upscaling is stuffing too much artificial intelligence into the creative process, leaving developers and players alike with a product that feels more like a corporate algorithm than a crafted experience. It’s a rare moment where an indie voice cuts through the usual tech hype, and the gaming community is listening—[even if Nvidia’s CEO Jensen Huang isn’t](https://www.theverge.com).


The backlash isn’t just about aesthetics. Oshry’s appeal taps into a growing unease among developers who feel squeezed between Nvidia’s aggressive AI push and their own artistic integrity. DLSS 5’s neural rendering promises smoother frame rates, but at what cost? Early adopters report [mushy textures and unnatural lighting](https://www.pcgamer.com), artifacts that make even high-budget titles look like they’ve been run through a Instagram filter. For a company that built its reputation on raw power, Nvidia’s pivot to AI feels like a bait-and-switch to some—a move that prioritizes stock-friendly buzzwords over the tactile joy of game art.


Jensen Huang’s response hasn’t helped. The Nvidia CEO has cycled through [defensive, conciliatory, and outright dismissive tones](https://www.wired.com) in recent weeks, a PR whiplash that’s only fueled the fire. When your flagship tech is being compared to a ‘neural gubbins’ by a respected studio head, maybe the problem isn’t the critics—it’s the product. The question now isn’t whether DLSS 5 works, but whether players even want it to.

<ArticleBreak />

The community’s reaction has been a mix of solidarity and skepticism. On forums like [ResetEra](https://www.resetera.com) and subreddits like r/pcgaming, players are debating whether a boycott could actually move the needle for a company as entrenched as Nvidia. Some argue that the real leverage lies with developers—if enough studios refuse to integrate DLSS 5, Nvidia might be forced to rethink its approach. Others point out that [AMD’s FSR 3](https://www.amd.com) offers a more transparent alternative, one that doesn’t rely on AI to paper over performance gaps. The divide isn’t just technical; it’s ideological. Do players want games that look *better*, or games that look *real*?


For now, the boycott remains more of a symbolic gesture than a coordinated movement. But the underlying tension is real. Nvidia’s AI push isn’t just about rendering—it’s about control. By making DLSS 5 the default for high-end gaming, the company is effectively dictating how games should look and feel, sidelining both developers and players in the process. The irony? Nvidia’s stock might not care about artistic integrity, but the people who actually play games do. And if enough of them vote with their wallets, even a tech giant might have to listen.


The real test will come with the next wave of AAA releases. If titles like *Starfield* or *Cyberpunk 2077*’s next-gen update lean heavily on DLSS 5, will players notice—or care? The answer could determine whether this backlash fizzles out or becomes a full-blown revolt. One thing’s certain: Nvidia’s AI art war isn’t just about pixels. It’s about who gets to decide what games look like in the first place.
