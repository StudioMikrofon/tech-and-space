There’s a quiet crisis in modern RPGs, and [Tim Cain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tim_Cain), co-creator of *Fallout*, just put his finger on it: **"A game that includes everything is about nothing."** It’s a truth so obvious it hurts—especially when you’re knee-deep in a 100-hour open-world slog where half the mechanics feel like they were added because some designer thought, *"Wouldn’t it be cool if…?"* without asking whether it actually *should*.

The problem isn’t ambition; it’s the delusion that more *always* equals better. Cain’s warning—[resurfaced in a *PC Gamer* piece](https://www.pcgamer.com/tim-cain-fallout-game-design-less-is-more/)—cuts to the heart of why so many AAA RPGs today feel like bloated checklists rather than cohesive experiences. Players aren’t just *saying* they’re overwhelmed; the data backs it up. Steam reviews for recent "kitchen sink" RPGs like [*Starfield*](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1716740/Starfield/) and [*Avowed*](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1217990/Avowed/) repeatedly ding them for "too many systems, not enough depth," a pattern that’s become so predictable it’s practically a genre trope. Even beloved titles aren’t immune: *Baldur’s Gate 3*’s [combat encounters](https://www.reddit.com/r/BaldursGate3/comments/17xz8kj/does_anyone_else_feel_like_the_combat_is_too/)—praised for their tactical depth—still spark debates about whether the game’s sheer volume of mechanics dilutes its storytelling punch.

Cain’s rule isn’t just about cutting features—it’s about *focus*. The most memorable moments in *Fallout*’s original games weren’t the result of a 50-page design doc; they emerged from constraints. When every dialogue option, weapon mod, or side quest *has* to justify its existence, the game starts to feel like a theme park where every ride is mandatory. **COMMUNITY PULSE**: The backlash isn’t just from "hardcore" players, either. Casual fans on [r/Games](https://www.reddit.com/r/Games/) and the *Fallout* subreddit increasingly describe feeling *guilty* for skipping content—a telltale sign that design has failed. As one top-voted comment put it: *"I don’t want to ‘engage with every system’; I want to feel like my choices matter."*

&lt;ArticleBreak />

So what’s the fix? Cain’s advice—don’t implement an idea just because it *sounds* cool—is easier said than done in an industry where trailers and press releases reward spectacle over substance. But the players who stick around aren’t the ones dazzled by bullet-pointed feature lists; they’re the ones who remember how *Fallout: New Vegas*’ [faction system](https://fallout.fandom.com/wiki/Fallout:_New_Vegas_factions) made their alliances feel *personal*, not procedural. Or how *Disco Elysium*’s [skill checks](https://www.pcgamer.com/disco-elysium-skill-checks/) turned stats into storytelling tools, not just numbers to min-max.

The friction point is clear: **PLAYER EXPECTATION** vs. **DEVELOPER AMBITION**. Gamers don’t actually want *less*—they want *better*. The same *PC Gamer* article notes that Cain’s rule applies to indie games too, where scope creep can kill projects before they even launch. But in AAA, the pressure is worse. When a game like [*The Elder Scrolls VI*](https://elderscrolls.bethesda.net/) gets announced, the hype cycle demands "bigger and more" by default. **BACKLASH RADAR**: The risk isn’t just that players will get bored; it’s that they’ll stop trusting studios to know what *actually* makes a game fun. We’ve already seen this with [*Skull and Bones*](https://www.metacritic.com/game/playstation-5/skull-and-bones/), where Ubisoft’s insistence on "more systems" led to a game that felt like a spreadsheet with a pirate hat.

The real test will be whether studios listen—or keep chasing the myth of the "perfect" game that does everything. Because as Cain’s rule reminds us, the games we remember aren’t the ones that tried to be everything. They’re the ones that knew exactly what they *weren’t*.
