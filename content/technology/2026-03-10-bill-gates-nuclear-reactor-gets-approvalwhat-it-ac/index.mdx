part1_en: "The Nuclear Regulatory Commission just handed Bill Gates’ [TerraPower](https://www.terrapower.com) a permit no other advanced nuclear startup has gotten: approval to build a commercial-scale reactor in Wyoming. This isn’t just another pilot project or a lab experiment—it’s the first new commercial reactor greenlit in the U.S. in nearly 50 years. The question isn’t whether this is a milestone (it is), but whether it’s a turning point for an industry that’s been stuck in regulatory and economic purgatory for decades.

The reactor, a sodium-cooled design called Natrium, promises to solve two of nuclear’s biggest pain points: cost and flexibility. Unlike traditional light-water reactors, Natrium is designed to ramp power output up or down to complement renewables, addressing the intermittency problem that’s dogged wind and solar. TerraPower claims it can [cut construction costs](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/us-department-energy-announces-80-million-advanced-nuclear-reactor-demonstrations) by using modular components and factory-built systems—though ‘cut’ is relative when nuclear’s price tag still starts in the billions.

But the real test isn’t the tech spec sheet; it’s whether this reactor can actually get built on time and on budget. The NRC’s approval is a critical step, but it’s not a blank check. TerraPower still needs to navigate supply chain bottlenecks (especially for the high-assay low-enriched uranium fuel it requires), local opposition in Wyoming, and the fact that [similar projects](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/us-nuclear-projects-face-delays-cost-overruns-gao-2023-09-12/)—like NuScale’s abandoned Utah plant—have collapsed under financial and logistical weight. Even with Gates’ deep pockets and the DOE’s $2 billion in backing, the path from permit to power isn’t smooth.

For the energy sector, this approval signals that the NRC is finally willing to move beyond its glacial pace for advanced reactors. That’s a win for startups like [Oklo](https://www.oklo.com) and [X-energy](https://x-energy.com), which have been waiting years for their own designs to get a green light. But it’s also a reminder that regulatory hurdles are just one piece of the puzzle. The bigger challenge? Proving that next-gen nuclear can deliver electricity cheaper and faster than the alternatives—gas, renewables plus storage, or even fusion down the line."

The Nuclear Regulatory Commission just handed Bill Gates’ [TerraPower](https://www.terrapower.com) a permit no other advanced nuclear startup has gotten: approval to build a commercial-scale reactor in Wyoming. This isn’t just another pilot project or a lab experiment—it’s the first new commercial reactor greenlit in the U.S. in nearly 50 years. The question isn’t whether this is a milestone (it is), but whether it’s a turning point for an industry that’s been stuck in regulatory and economic purgatory for decades.

The reactor, a sodium-cooled design called Natrium, promises to solve two of nuclear’s biggest pain points: cost and flexibility. Unlike traditional light-water reactors, Natrium is designed to ramp power output up or down to complement renewables, addressing the intermittency problem that’s dogged wind and solar. TerraPower claims it can [cut construction costs](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/us-department-energy-announces-80-million-advanced-nuclear-reactor-demonstrations) by using modular components and factory-built systems—though ‘cut’ is relative when nuclear’s price tag still starts in the billions.

But the real test isn’t the tech spec sheet; it’s whether this reactor can actually get built on time and on budget. The NRC’s approval is a critical step, but it’s not a blank check. TerraPower still needs to navigate supply chain bottlenecks (especially for the high-assay low-enriched uranium fuel it requires), local opposition in Wyoming, and the fact that [similar projects](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/us-nuclear-projects-face-delays-cost-overruns-gao-2023-09-12/)—like NuScale’s abandoned Utah plant—have collapsed under financial and logistical weight. Even with Gates’ deep pockets and the DOE’s $2 billion in backing, the path from permit to power isn’t smooth.

For the energy sector, this approval signals that the NRC is finally willing to move beyond its glacial pace for advanced reactors. That’s a win for startups like [Oklo](https://www.oklo.com) and [X-energy](https://x-energy.com), which have been waiting years for their own designs to get a green light. But it’s also a reminder that regulatory hurdles are just one piece of the puzzle. The bigger challenge? Proving that next-gen nuclear can deliver electricity cheaper and faster than the alternatives—gas, renewables plus storage, or even fusion down the line.

<ArticleBreak />

So who should care about this? For utilities and grid operators, Natrium’s load-following capability is the headline feature. A reactor that can dial output down to 20% of capacity—without the efficiency losses of traditional plants—could make nuclear a better partner for renewables. That’s critical in states like Wyoming, where coal plants are retiring and [wind farms](https://www.eia.gov/state/?sid=WY) already supply a third of the power. But the catch is that TerraPower’s first plant won’t even come online until 2030, assuming no delays. By then, battery storage and green hydrogen might have eaten into nuclear’s niche.

For the climate math, the stakes are higher. The U.S. needs to [double or triple](https://www.iea.org/reports/nuclear-power-in-a-clean-energy-system) its nuclear capacity by 2050 to hit net-zero targets, but the industry has been in retreat for years. TerraPower’s win could revive investor interest, but it’s competing against a brutal reality: even with subsidies, new nuclear is [still more expensive](https://www.lazard.com/perspective/lcoe2023/) than utility-scale solar or wind. The bet here is that advanced reactors will eventually undercut those costs—but ‘eventually’ doesn’t pay the bills for ratepayers or shareholders.

Then there’s the fuel problem. Natrium runs on HALEU, a fuel the U.S. doesn’t produce at scale. Right now, TerraPower is [relying on Russia](https://www.politico.com/news/2023/12/12/nuclear-fuel-russia-sanctions-00131042/) for its supply, which is politically untenable long-term. The DOE is racing to stand up domestic production, but until that happens, the reactor’s timeline is hostage to geopolitics. It’s a microcosm of nuclear’s broader dilemma: the tech is promising, but the ecosystem around it—fuel, regulation, financing—is still fragile.

What’s missing from the hype is the user perspective. For most Americans, this approval changes nothing today. Electricity prices won’t drop overnight, and Wyoming’s grid won’t suddenly get cleaner. But if TerraPower succeeds, it could finally give utilities a zero-carbon option that doesn’t require betting the farm on unproven storage tech or praying for a fusion breakthrough. The real test isn’t whether the reactor works—it’s whether it works *better* than the alternatives by the time it’s plugged in.
