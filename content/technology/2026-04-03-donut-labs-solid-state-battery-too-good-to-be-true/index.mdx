Donut Lab’s January announcement didn’t just promise a solid-state battery—it promised *the* solid-state battery. A cell that charges in minutes, lasts hundreds of years, and uses [cheap, abundant materials](https://www.engadget.com/whats-going-on-with-donut-lab-140034217.html) like sodium and carbon. If confirmed, it would obliterate the trade-offs plaguing today’s lithium-ion tech: slow charging, degradation, and reliance on rare metals. But the louder the claims, the louder the skepticism.


The battery industry has spent decades chasing solid-state designs, with giants like [QuantumScape](https://www.quantumscape.com/) and [Toyota](https://global.toyota/en/newsroom/corporate/39130827.html) inching toward commercial viability—yet even their timelines stretch to 2027 or later. Donut Lab, a Finnish-Estonian startup with no prior commercial product, claims to have leapfrogged them all. That’s either a miracle of engineering or a red flag.


Critics point to the lack of peer-reviewed data or third-party validation. The company’s [demo videos](https://twitter.com/DonutLabs) show rapid charging, but without independent testing, it’s impossible to verify longevity claims. Meanwhile, the materials story—sodium and carbon—sounds plausible on paper, but scaling production is another matter entirely.

<ArticleBreak />

For users, the stakes are simple: a battery that lasts decades and recharges faster than a coffee break would upend everything from smartphones to electric vehicles. No more range anxiety, no more replacement cycles. But the industry’s reaction has been muted, with analysts noting that [solid-state batteries](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41560-023-01203-8) typically trade one compromise for another—high energy density for slow charging, or fast charging for shorter lifespans. Donut Lab claims to have cracked all of them at once.


The real test isn’t the lab; it’s the supply chain. Even if the tech works, ramping up production of a radically new battery chemistry would take years and billions in investment. Compare that to [CATL’s sodium-ion batteries](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/chinas-catl-unveils-first-generation-sodium-ion-battery-2021-07-29/), which are already in limited commercial use but offer far modest gains. Donut Lab’s timeline—if it even exists—remains a mystery.


Then there’s the question of who benefits. If this battery is as cheap and durable as claimed, it could democratize energy storage, undercutting lithium mining giants and reshaping geopolitical supply chains. Or it could join the graveyard of [overhyped energy breakthroughs](https://www.technologyreview.com/2023/01/04/1066000/the-long-fuse-of-the-energy-revolution/), leaving users with the same old trade-offs.
