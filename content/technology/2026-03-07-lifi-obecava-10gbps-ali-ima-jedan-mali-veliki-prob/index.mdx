For years, LiFi—the wireless data transmission tech that uses light waves instead of radio frequencies—has been pitched as the answer to crowded Wi-Fi bands and security vulnerabilities in public networks. Now, [pureLiFi’s latest system](https://www.purelifi.com/news/purelifi-launches-worlds-first-lifi-certified-system-delivering-10gbps-speeds/) delivers on the speed front, offering **10Gbps** throughput with a plug-and-play setup aimed at offices, hospitals, and industrial sites. The catch? It only works when your device has a direct, unobstructed line of sight to the LiFi access point. Move your laptop, shift in your chair, or—god forbid—stand up to grab a coffee, and the connection drops like a bad Zoom call in 2020.

This isn’t a flaw in pureLiFi’s execution; it’s a fundamental constraint of physics. Light doesn’t bend around corners or penetrate walls, which means LiFi’s real-world deployment looks less like a seamless upgrade and more like a high-maintenance niche solution. The company frames it as a **‘complement to Wi-Fi’**, not a replacement, targeting scenarios where RF interference is a dealbreaker (think MRI rooms or petrochemical plants) or where data security demands air-gapped networks. But for the average office worker, the trade-off is stark: blazing speeds in exchange for sitting perfectly still under a desk lamp.

The timing is ironic. As enterprises grapple with hybrid work and hot-desking, LiFi’s line-of-sight requirement feels like a throwback to the era of wired Ethernet—except now, the ‘cable’ is an invisible beam you can’t trip over but can easily block. Early adopters in [healthcare and defense](https://www.techradar.com/news/lifi-could-be-the-future-of-wireless-but-it-has-one-big-problem) have tolerated these limits for years, but pureLiFi’s consumer-friendly pitch risks colliding with a market that’s moved on to expecting mobility as a baseline. Even the [IEEE 802.11bb standard](https://standards.ieee.org/ieee/802.11bb/7760/), ratified last year to govern LiFi interoperability, can’t solve the fact that humans aren’t statues.

Meanwhile, Wi-Fi isn’t standing still. Wi-Fi 7’s **multi-link operation** and **320MHz channels** are pushing real-world speeds past 5Gbps in ideal conditions, with none of LiFi’s positional fuss. The [Wireless Broadband Alliance](https://wballiance.com/) is already certifying devices for the next wave of upgrades, and mesh systems from the likes of [TP-Link and Netgear](https://www.theverge.com/23633492/wi-fi-7-router-2024-best-asus-netgear-tp-link) are making dead zones a relic. LiFi’s advantage in latency and security remains, but for most users, ‘good enough’ Wi-Fi is getting harder to disrupt.

PureLiFi’s bet hinges on two assumptions: that enterprises will prioritize security and speed over convenience, and that the tech’s limitations can be engineered around. The company’s [Kitefin module](https://www.purelifi.com/products/kitefin/), a USB-C dongle that adds LiFi to laptops, is a step toward broader compatibility, but it’s an extra cost—and another thing to lose. For now, the sweet spot seems to be fixed-workstation environments like trading floors or surgical suites, where devices rarely move and RF interference is a non-starter. Even there, IT teams must contend with **light pollution**: overhead fluorescents or sunlight can swamp the signal, requiring careful lighting design.

The bigger question is whether LiFi’s strengths justify its constraints in a world where **5G mmWave** and **Wi-Fi 7** are closing the gap on latency and throughput. In [industrial IoT](https://www.iotforall.com/lifi-vs-wifi-for-industrial-iot), LiFi’s immunity to electromagnetic interference makes it a contender for factory floors, but competing standards like [Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN)](https://www.ieee802.org/1/pages/tsn.html) are also vying for that space. For consumers, the calculus is simpler: no one wants to rearrange their furniture for faster internet. PureLiFi’s plug-and-play branding obscures the fact that ‘plug-and-pray’ might be more accurate—unless your idea of plug-and-play includes a **site survey to map light paths**.

There’s also the issue of ecosystem lock-in. LiFi’s hardware dependencies (specialized access points, dongles, or integrated chips) mean early adopters are betting on a standard that’s still finding its feet. By contrast, Wi-Fi’s ubiquity is self-reinforcing: every laptop, phone, and smart fridge ships with it built in. PureLiFi is [partnering with chipmakers](https://www.lightreading.com/enterprise-iot/purelifi-partners-with-onyx-to-bring-lifi-to-more-devices) to embed LiFi into devices, but until it’s as invisible as Bluetooth, the friction remains.

The most plausible near-term role for LiFi is as a **high-speed backhaul** in hybrid networks, where it handles fixed connections (like desktop PCs or VR headsets) while Wi-Fi manages mobile devices. That’s a far cry from the ‘Wi-Fi killer’ hype of a decade ago, but it’s a pragmatic niche. The real test will come when pureLiFi’s [‘LiFi-as-a-service’](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/purelifi-launches-lifi-as-a-service/) model rolls out later this year, putting the tech’s operational costs—and user tolerance—under the microscope.
