The campaign hinges on a calculated redefinition. By labeling data center hardware, servers, and networking gear as *critical infrastructure*, lobbyists are pushing to exempt them from Colorado’s [right-to-repair law](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb22-003), which currently requires manufacturers to provide tools, parts, and documentation to independent repair shops. The argument? Unauthorized repairs could introduce security vulnerabilities—a claim that [repair advocates call fearmongering](https://www.404media.co/cisco-ibm-lobbyists-colorado-right-to-repair-law-exemptions/) without evidence.


   For IT admins and small businesses, the stakes are immediate. A server farm’s failed power supply or a router’s fried circuit board often can’t wait for OEM-approved turnaround times. Independent repair shops, which handle an estimated [30–40% of enterprise hardware fixes](https://www.ifixit.com/News/55000/right-to-repair-wins-in-colorado), warn that exemptions would force reliance on manufacturer monopolies—driving up costs and downtime.


   The playbook isn’t new. Tech giants have long resisted repair rights by invoking [safety concerns](https://www.theverge.com/2023/5/10/23717302/right-to-repair-apple-google-microsoft-lobbying) or [intellectual property](https://www.eff.org/issues/right-to-repair), but the ‘critical infrastructure’ angle is a fresh twist. It reframes a consumer protection issue as a matter of national resilience, despite no publicized breaches tied to third-party repairs.

<ArticleBreak />

The irony? Many of these same companies already sell ‘authorized’ repair services at a premium. Cisco’s [Smart Net Total Care](https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/services/technical/smart-net-total-care/index.html) contracts, for example, can cost up to 20% of a device’s purchase price annually—while independent shops charge a fraction for the same work. The lobbying effort, then, isn’t just about security; it’s about preserving a lucrative service ecosystem.


   Smaller players are caught in the crossfire. ‘We’ve built our business on fixing what vendors won’t,’ says [Denver-based IT repair shop owner Mark Chen](https://www.linkedin.com/in/markchen-it/), who notes that 60% of his revenue comes from data center hardware. ‘If this passes, we’re either out of a job or forced to operate in a legal gray zone.’


   The broader risk is a domino effect. Colorado’s law is a model for [similar bills in 20+ states](https://www.repair.org/stand-up). If ‘critical infrastructure’ exemptions stick here, expect them to spread—leaving hospitals, banks, and cloud providers with fewer options and higher bills.
