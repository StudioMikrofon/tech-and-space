For seven decades, binary logic has been computing’s unshakable foundation. Every CPU in your phone, laptop, or car crunches 1s and 0s because, frankly, it’s *easy*: cheap to manufacture, simple to design, and backed by trillions in infrastructure. So when a [ternary RISC processor](https://hackaday.com/2023/11/ternary-risc-processor-achieves-non-binary-computing-via-fpga/)—using three states (1, 0, and -1) instead of two—emerged from an FPGA prototype, the natural question wasn’t *how*, but *why now*?

The pitch for ternary is seductive on paper. Three states mean more information per clock cycle, which *could* translate to lower power use or faster parallel math—useful for [AI acceleration](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.03401) or signal processing. The FPGA implementation, while not a commercial product, proves the concept works outside labs. Yet here’s the catch: binary’s dominance isn’t technical. It’s economic. Foundries are tooled for 0s and 1s; compilers assume them; even [quantum computing](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06092-1) often defaults to binary qubits for compatibility.

Early adopters might include niche embedded systems where power efficiency justifies the overhead. But for 99% of developers, ternary today is like [RISC-V in 2015](https://riscv.org/about/history/): theoretically elegant, practically irrelevant until the toolchain catches up. The real test isn’t whether ternary *can* work—it’s whether anyone will bother rewriting the rulebook for it.

<ArticleBreak />

The community reaction so far? Polite applause, zero urgency. [Hackaday commenters](https://hackaday.com/2023/11/ternary-risc-processor-achieves-non-binary-computing-via-fpga/#comments) praise the hacker spirit but note the lack of a ‘killer app’—no task where ternary is *obviously* better, not just *theoretically* cleaner. Even the FPGA route, while flexible, highlights the problem: if ternary were a clear win, why isn’t TSMC or Intel [betting silicon on it](https://semiwiki.com/semiconductor-manufacturers/tsmc/)?

Then there’s the ecosystem tax. Binary’s inertia is a moat. Every IDE, every OS kernel, every GPU shader assumes two states. Porting that stack to ternary would require a [decade of coordinated effort](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3579371)—or a crisis (like the end of Moore’s Law) to force the issue. The closest parallel is [analog computing’s brief revival](https://spectrum.ieee.org/analog-computing) for AI, where efficiency gains justified the pain. Ternary lacks that narrative.

What *does* work here? The FPGA prototype is a low-risk sandbox for researchers to stress-test ternary’s limits. If a breakthrough emerges—say, a 20% power saving in neural nets—*then* the industry might listen. Until then, this is a footnote in computing’s ‘what if’ file, not a turning point. The signal isn’t the tech; it’s the silence around it.
