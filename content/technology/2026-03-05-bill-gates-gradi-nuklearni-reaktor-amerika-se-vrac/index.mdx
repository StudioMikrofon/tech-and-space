The Nuclear Regulatory Commission just handed Bill Gates’ TerraPower a permit to build the first commercial advanced nuclear reactor in the US in nearly 50 years. On paper, this is the kind of milestone that should reset expectations for America’s energy future: a next-gen reactor, designed to run on high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU), promising safer operation and less waste than traditional plants. The [Natrium reactor](https://www.terrapower.com/our-technology/natrium-technology/), slated for Wyoming, even includes molten salt storage—a feature that could theoretically smooth out intermittency issues plaguing renewables.

But permits don’t generate electricity, and the hard questions start now. The project’s $4 billion price tag—split between TerraPower, the Department of Energy, and private backers like [Warren Buffett’s PacifiCorp](https://www.pacificorp.com/about-us/newsroom/news-releases/2024/240716-terrapower.html)—is just the down payment. The real cost will be measured in decades of operational proof, fuel supply chains that don’t yet exist at scale, and a regulatory framework still catching up to advanced designs. For an industry that’s spent years promising ‘nuclear renaissance’ only to deliver delays and cost overruns (see: [Vogtle’s $30B budget explosion](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/georgia-power-vogtle-nuclear-plant-cost-overruns/652340/)), this approval is less a finish line than a starting gun.

The competitive landscape isn’t waiting. While TerraPower navigates the [HALEU fuel shortage](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/biden-harris-administration-takes-major-step-strengthen-domestic-nuclear-fuel)—currently reliant on downblended Russian stockpiles—the likes of [NuScale](https://www.nuscale.com/) (scaled-back SMRs), [Oklo](https://www.oklo.com/) (micro-reactors), and even fusion upstarts like [Helion](https://helionenergy.com/) are jockeying for position. The difference? Most aren’t betting on gigawatt-scale plants. TerraPower’s Natrium is a 345 MW beast, sized for grid impact but requiring grid-level commitment. That’s a tough sell in a market where utilities are still burned by the last nuclear boom-and-bust cycle.

For end users—be they ratepayers in Wyoming or corporate buyers like Microsoft (which [inked a power purchase agreement](https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2023/09/27/microsoft-signs-power-purchase-agreement-with-terrapower-for-advanced-nuclear-energy/) with TerraPower)—the practical impact hinges on two numbers: cost per kilowatt-hour and construction timeline. The company targets 2030 for operation, but nuclear projects [routinely miss deadlines](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=51956) by years. If Natrium delivers on its $89/MWh estimate, it could undercut gas in some markets. If it doesn’t, it’s just another expensive lesson in energy transition growing pains.

The user reality here isn’t about reactors—it’s about risk allocation. TerraPower’s backers are betting that advanced nuclear can avoid the pitfalls of its predecessors: no meltdowns (thanks to passive safety systems), no fuel scarcity (once HALEU production ramps up), and no public backlash (a tall order in post-Fukushima America). But the [community reaction](https://www.casperstartribune.net/news/energy/terrapower-nuclear-reactor-wyoming-approval/article_1a2b3c4d-5e6f-4g7h-8i9j-k0l1m2n3o4p5.html) in Kemmerer, Wyoming—where the reactor will replace a coal plant—has been cautiously optimistic, not euphoric. Locals remember the [coal industry’s collapse](https://www.eenews.net/articles/coal-country-braces-for-another-blow-as-plants-close/) and aren’t banking on nuclear as a jobs panacea.

What works in TerraPower’s favor is its hybrid approach. The Natrium design pairs the reactor with a molten salt storage system that can discharge power for up to 5.5 hours, effectively acting as a giant battery. That’s a direct answer to renewable energy’s Achilles’ heel: storage. If it performs as advertised, it could make nuclear a viable partner for wind and solar, not just a competitor. The catch? Molten salt tech is [proven but not perfected](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/molten-salt-reactors-and-their-challenges)—corrosion and material science hurdles remain.

The bigger picture is that this permit doesn’t exist in a vacuum. The Inflation Reduction Act’s nuclear production tax credits (up to [$30/MWh](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-23-40.pdf)) make projects like Natrium financially viable on paper, but the credits expire in 2032—before the plant is even scheduled to open. That’s a gamble on Congress extending support, a bet that’s far from certain in today’s political climate. Meanwhile, the [DOE’s $1.5B loan guarantee](https://www.energy.gov/lpo/articles/doe-announces-conditional-commitment-15-billion-loan-guarantee-advanced-nuclear) for TerraPower is a lifeline, but it’s also a reminder that advanced nuclear still can’t stand on its own two feet commercially.

The most honest takeaway isn’t about the technology—it’s about the timeline. If Natrium comes online by 2030, it’ll be competing with a very different grid than today’s: one with cheaper renewables, maturing long-duration storage, and possibly even early fusion pilots. The question isn’t whether advanced nuclear *can* work, but whether it can work *fast enough* to matter. For now, this permit is a necessary step, not a sufficient one.
