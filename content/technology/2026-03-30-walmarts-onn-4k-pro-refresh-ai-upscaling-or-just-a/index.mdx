Walmart’s [Onn 4K Pro](https://www.walmart.com/ip/ONN-4K-Pro-Streaming-Device/50042600) has quietly become a sleeper hit in the budget streaming box wars—now leaks suggest a sequel with a [6nm chip](https://www.anandtech.com/show/17052/the-6nm-process-node-overview) and AI upscaling. According to [Android Authority’s report](https://www.androidauthority.com/walmart-onn-4k-pro-2-3314212/), the move targets efficiency first, a rare priority in a segment obsessed with raw specs. For users drowning in remote controls and laggy interfaces, a cooler-running box that sips power could matter more than another incremental resolution bump.


Yet the AI upscaling claim deserves skepticism. Even high-end TVs struggle to make 720p content look like native 4K without artifacts, and budget hardware rarely delivers on such promises. The real test isn’t whether the Onn can *claim* AI enhancements—it’s whether it avoids the [soap-opera effect](https://www.rtings.com/tv/learn/soap-opera-effect) that plagues cheaper upscalers. Walmart’s play here isn’t about out-featuring Roku or Fire TV; it’s about undercutting them while closing the experience gap.


This isn’t a specs arms race. It’s a cost-of-ownership game. A more efficient chip means less heat, longer lifespan, and maybe even a lower price point—factors that actually move the needle for the [42% of U.S. households](https://www.leichtmanresearch.com/press/42-of-u-s-households-have-a-connected-tv-device/) relying on budget streamers. The question isn’t whether Walmart can match premium brands on paper, but whether it can make ‘good enough’ *better* for the masses.

<ArticleBreak />

The competitive pressure here is subtle but real. Roku and Amazon’s Fire TV dominate with ecosystem lock-in—apps, voice assistants, and brand loyalty—but Walmart’s [house-brand strategy](https://corporate.walmart.com/newsroom/working-with-suppliers/private-brands) chips away at the edges. A $30 box with 80% of the performance is a tough sell for casual users to ignore, especially when paired with Walmart’s physical retail reach. The 6nm chip, if confirmed, would be a first for this price tier, forcing rivals to justify their margins.


That said, AI upscaling in a budget device is a classic [‘checkbox feature’](https://www.theverge.com/23647462/tech-specs-sheet-marketing-gimmicks-ces-2023)—something to list in ads but rarely used in practice. Most streaming content is already 1080p or 4K, and the heavy lifting happens on the server side (see: Netflix’s [dynamic optimization](https://netflixtechblog.com/per-title-encode-optimization-7e99442b62a2)). The real user benefit? A box that doesn’t throttle during marathon sessions or turn into a hand warmer. Efficiency isn’t sexy, but it’s what keeps budget hardware out of landfills.


The bigger story might be Walmart’s growing confidence in tech. From [ONN tablets](https://www.theverge.com/2022/11/15/23459902/walmart-onn-tablet-android-12-kids-parental-controls) to [Vizio TVs](https://www.cnet.com/tech/home-entertainment/walmart-is-selling-a-55-inch-4k-tv-for-228-heres-the-catch/), the retailer is methodically building a portfolio that competes on value, not hype. If this refresh delivers on efficiency without botching the basics (like [HDR support](https://www.rtings.com/tv/reviews/by-usage/movies/hdr)), it could redefine ‘good enough’ for millions of users who don’t care about Dolby Vision but *do* care about stutter-free playback.

For cord-cutters tired of upgrading hardware every two years, this could be the rare ‘set it and forget it’ option. The catch? Walmart’s update cycle remains unpredictable. If history repeats, this box might get one major firmware update before being abandoned—leaving early adopters to weigh short-term gains against long-term support.
