Electric vehicle owners in at least a dozen states may soon pay **$200 to $250 annually** in new fees—**two to three times** what the average gas-powered car contributes via federal fuel taxes. The justification? Funding crumbling roads. The problem? The math is a political sleight of hand.

  Gas taxes, currently **18.4 cents per gallon federally**, generate about **$70–$100 per year** for the typical driver, according to [Congressional Budget Office data](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/57021). Yet EVs, which account for [roughly 10% of new car sales](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-07-12/us-electric-vehicle-sales-hit-record-7-2-in-q2-2023) and weigh far less (reducing road wear), face flat fees that assume they’re the budget black hole. Lawmakers frame this as ‘user pays’ equity, but it’s more like ‘user *over*pays’—especially when EVs already [deliver $1.7 billion annually in public health benefits](https://www.ucsusa.org/resources/cleaner-cars-cleaner-air) by cutting tailpipe pollution.

  The timing is particularly tone-deaf. With federal EV tax credits finally gaining traction and automakers like [Tesla and Ford](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/ford-tesla-deal-gives-blue-oval-customers-access-supercharger-network-2023-05-25/) racing to expand charging networks, these fees risk **undermining adoption just as it hits critical mass**. Worse, they ignore the **second-order cost**: if EVs stall at 15% market share because of punitive policies, the U.S. misses its [2030 emissions targets](https://www.epa.gov/regulations-emissions-vehicles-and-engines/epa-and-dot-propose-new-emissions-standards-light)—and gas drivers foot the bill for *those* failures later.

<ArticleBreak />

The real kicker? **EVs already pay for roads—just differently.** Registration fees, sales taxes, and (in some states) **per-kWh charges** cover infrastructure. The issue isn’t that EVs freeload; it’s that gas taxes, unchanged since 1993, no longer reflect inflation or fuel efficiency. Instead of modernizing the system—say, with **mileage-based fees** that apply to *all* vehicles—lawmakers are slapping EVs with a **regressive surcharge** that treats them like luxury items.

  Industry players see the hypocrisy. [Rivian’s policy team](https://rivian.com/policy) notes that ‘flat fees disproportionately hurt rural EV owners,’ who often drive longer distances but lack charging alternatives. Meanwhile, gas guzzlers in [heavy SUVs](https://www.epa.gov/automotive-trends/highlights-automotive-trends-report) (now 80% of U.S. sales) keep paying pennies per mile while doing **10x the pavement damage** of a Model 3.

  The broader signal is clear: **this isn’t about roads—it’s about protecting gas revenue.** States like [Texas and Ohio](https://www.ncsl.org/transportation/electric-vehicle-fees), which have passed similar fees, also happen to be [top oil and gas producers](https://www.eia.gov/state/). Coincidence? Maybe. But when ‘infrastructure funding’ starts looking like **a sin tax on progress**, even fiscal conservatives should ask: *Why penalize the cars that save us money on healthcare and foreign oil?*

  For now, the fees are a **test balloon**. If they stick, expect two outcomes: **slower EV adoption in red states**, and a **patchwork of conflicting policies** that make cross-country road trips a bureaucratic nightmare. The only winners? Lobbyists and the status quo.

Drugim riječima, this isn’t a funding fix—it’s a **tax on transition**. The same states screaming about ‘fairness’ are the ones clinging to a 30-year-old gas tax model that rewards inefficiency. The cognitive dissonance would be impressive if it weren’t so expensive for the rest of us.
