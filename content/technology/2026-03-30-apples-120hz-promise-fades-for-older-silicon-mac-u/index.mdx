Apple’s new [Studio Display XDR](https://www.apple.com/studio-display/) arrives with a catch: its marquee 120Hz refresh rate is reserved for Macs with M2, M3, or the highest-tier M1 chips. Older Apple Silicon models—including the base M1 MacBook Air and 13-inch MacBook Pro—are capped at 60Hz, according to [Apple’s support pages](https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT213495). That’s not just a number on a spec sheet; it’s a tangible downgrade for motion clarity, scrolling smoothness, and pro workflows like video editing or 3D modeling where high refresh rates matter.


The omission stings more because Apple’s marketing leans hard on the XDR label, a term borrowed from its $5,000 Pro Display XDR. Yet here, the ‘pro’ experience is gated by silicon age, not just price. Even the cheaper [Studio Display](https://www.apple.com/studio-display/) (non-XDR) faces the same 60Hz limit on older chips, though its 60Hz panel makes the trade-off less jarring.


Intel Macs, meanwhile, aren’t even invited to the party. Apple’s support docs don’t list compatibility for any Intel-based machine, effectively cutting off a swath of users still clinging to 2019–2020 models. For studios or offices mixing old and new hardware, this isn’t just an upgrade nudge—it’s a forced migration.

<ArticleBreak />

The practical impact lands unevenly. Casual users on an M1 MacBook Air won’t notice 60Hz for emails or spreadsheets, but creatives using [Final Cut Pro](https://www.apple.com/final-cut-pro/) or [Adobe After Effects](https://www.adobe.com/products/aftereffects.html) will feel the drag in timeline scrubbing and playback. Apple’s own [ProMotion](https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT212947) tech, a selling point for iPads and iPhones, now highlights a fragmentation in its desktop ecosystem: the same display behaves differently depending on which Mac it’s plugged into.


This isn’t just about hardware limits—it’s a strategic push. By tying 120Hz to newer chips, Apple incentivizes upgrades while quietly phasing out Intel support. Competitors like [LG’s UltraFine 5K](https://www.lg.com/us/monitors/lg-27md5kl-b) or [Dell’s UltraSharp](https://www.dell.com/en-us/shop/dell-ultrasharp-27-4k-usb-c-monitor-u2723qe/apd/210-bdjr/monitors-monitor-accessories) don’t impose such restrictions, but they lack the seamless macOS integration Apple offers. The trade-off? A walled garden where ‘it just works’—until it doesn’t.


The bigger question is whether this becomes a pattern. If Apple’s [M4 chips](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-02-14/apple-s-m4-chip-production-reportedly-kicks-off-for-new-macs-ipads) later this year bring *another* tier of display performance, early adopters of M1 and M2 Macs could face a similar downgrade cycle. For pros investing in long-term setups, that’s a risky bet.
