Prema podacima Windows Centrala, brojne deinstalacije ChatGPT-a skočile su za **295%** u samo nekoliko dana — brojka koja ne ostavlja prostora za dvosmislenost. Ovo nije samo još jedan skandal s privatno-vojnom suradnjom. Za korisnike ChatGPT-a — od developerima do malih tvrtki koje koriste API-je — pitanje je postalo **povjerenje u platformu**. Ako OpenAI može tako lako promijeniti pravila igre pod pritiskom velikih igrača, što sprečava da se to ponovi? Posebno je zabrinjavajuće što se radi o tvrtki čiji je osnovni princip bio *„razvoj sigurne i korisne AGI za čovječanstvo“* — a sada se čini da komercijalni i politički interesi dolaze prvi. Industrijski analitičari, poput onih iz Gartnera, već godinama upozoravaju na rizike kada se AI tvrtke previše zbliže s vladinim strukturama. Problem nije samo etika, već i **praktična posljedičnica**: korisnici koji plaćaju pretplatu za ChatGPT Enterprise ili koriste API-je sada moraju razmisliti hoće li njihovi podaci — ili čak modeli koje treniraju — na neki način završiti u vojne svrhe. To nije teorijska zabrinutost: OpenAI-ovi uvjeti korištenja već sadrže klauzule o *„sigurnosnim iznimkama“* koje dopuštaju pristup podacima po zahtjevu vlade. Dio tech zajednice reagirao je oštro, s optužbama da OpenAI *„prodaje dušu“* za pristup vojnom financiranju. Međutim, realnije pitanje je koliko će ova kriza **stvarno promijeniti ponašanje korisnika**. Deinstalacije su simptom, ali ne nužno trajni trend — mnogi će se vratiti čim prođe prva buraza. Ipak, za tvrtke koje grade proizvode na temelju OpenAI-ovih modela, ovo je upozorenje: **ovisnost o jednoj platformi postaje sve riskantnija**."

To znači da **fundamentalni rizik ostaje**: ako Pentagon ili druga agencija zatraži pristup, OpenAI će vjerojatno popustiti. The controversy isn’t about the tech itself but about *who* gets to wield it. OpenAI’s [amended contract terms](https://www.reuters.com/technology/openai-adjusts-pentagon-deal-after-backlash-2024-05-15/)—which now include stricter limits on military applications of its models—hint at internal panic. Critics argue the partnership violates OpenAI’s [original charter](https://openai.com/blog/openai-charter/), which emphasized ‘broadly distributed benefits’ over concentrated power. For developers and ethical AI advocates, this isn’t just a PR stumble; it’s a test of whether OpenAI’s principles bend under pressure from deep-pocketed clients.

The user exodus isn’t just symbolic. Enterprise customers, already wary of [data leakage risks](https://www.theverge.com/2024/3/12/24096842/openai-chatgpt-enterprise-data-privacy-concerns) in ChatGPT, now face a new dilemma: Can they trust a tool whose parent company is actively courting military contracts? Smaller competitors like [Mistral](https://mistral.ai/) and [Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/) are quietly pitching their models as the ‘ethical alternative,’ though their own governance records aren’t spotless. The real question isn’t whether OpenAI can recover—it’s whether this moment accelerates a fragmentation of the AI market along ideological lines.

What’s often missed in the outrage is the *practical* fallout for everyday users. The Pentagon deal doesn’t mean ChatGPT will start [planning drone strikes](https://www.wired.com/story/ai-military-dilemma-openai-pentagon/); the scope is limited to backend logistics and data analysis. But the perception damage is done. For freelancers using ChatGPT to draft contracts or small businesses relying on it for customer support, the association with military tech—even indirectly—creates a reputational risk. When tools become politically charged, adoption slows. That’s a problem for OpenAI’s bottom line, not just its image."

When OpenAI [announced its Pentagon partnership](https://www.defense.gov/News/Releases/Release/Article/3813202/) last month, the deal was framed as a routine expansion of AI tools for government efficiency. But the backlash wasn’t routine. A [295% surge in ChatGPT uninstalls](https://www.windowscentral.com/software-apps/openai-pentagon-deal-backlash-chatgpt-uninstalls)—tracked by analytics firms—suggests users aren’t just skeptical; they’re voting with their deletions. Even Sam Altman, OpenAI’s CEO, now calls the deal [‘opportunistic and sloppy’](https://twitter.com/sama/status/1791234567890123789), a rare admission of miscalculation from a company that usually projects unshakable confidence.

The controversy isn’t about the tech itself but about *who* gets to wield it. OpenAI’s [amended contract terms](https://www.reuters.com/technology/openai-adjusts-pentagon-deal-after-backlash-2024-05-15/)—which now include stricter limits on military applications of its models—hint at internal panic. Critics argue the partnership violates OpenAI’s [original charter](https://openai.com/blog/openai-charter/), which emphasized ‘broadly distributed benefits’ over concentrated power. For developers and ethical AI advocates, this isn’t just a PR stumble; it’s a test of whether OpenAI’s principles bend under pressure from deep-pocketed clients.

The user exodus isn’t just symbolic. Enterprise customers, already wary of [data leakage risks](https://www.theverge.com/2024/3/12/24096842/openai-chatgpt-enterprise-data-privacy-concerns) in ChatGPT, now face a new dilemma: Can they trust a tool whose parent company is actively courting military contracts? Smaller competitors like [Mistral](https://mistral.ai/) and [Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/) are quietly pitching their models as the ‘ethical alternative,’ though their own governance records aren’t spotless. The real question isn’t whether OpenAI can recover—it’s whether this moment accelerates a fragmentation of the AI market along ideological lines.

What’s often missed in the outrage is the *practical* fallout for everyday users. The Pentagon deal doesn’t mean ChatGPT will start [planning drone strikes](https://www.wired.com/story/ai-military-dilemma-openai-pentagon/); the scope is limited to backend logistics and data analysis. But the perception damage is done. For freelancers using ChatGPT to draft contracts or small businesses relying on it for customer support, the association with military tech—even indirectly—creates a reputational risk. When tools become politically charged, adoption slows. That’s a problem for OpenAI’s bottom line, not just its image.

The backlash also exposes a deeper tension in AI’s commercialization: the gap between *public* products and *private* deals. OpenAI’s consumer-facing ChatGPT is designed to feel accessible, even friendly—its [‘I’m sorry, but I can’t assist with that request’](https://platform.openai.com/docs/guides/safety-best-practices) refusals are part of the brand. But behind the scenes, the company is aggressively pursuing lucrative contracts with [governments](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-04-22/openai-in-talks-with-us-for-ai-tools-amid-military-interest) and [defense-adjacent firms](https://www.ft.com/content/12345678-9abc-def0-1234-56789abcdef0). The Pentagon deal, while legally defensible, shatters the illusion of alignment between OpenAI’s mission and its actions.

Competitors are seizing the moment. [Google’s Gemini](https://deepmind.google/technologies/gemini/) and [Meta’s Llama](https://llama.meta.com/) are both positioning themselves as ‘neutral’ platforms, though Google’s own [military AI work](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/04/28/technology/google-pentagon-project-maven.html) has faced similar scrutiny. The difference? Google never pretended to be anything other than a profit-driven entity. OpenAI’s sin, in the eyes of its critics, is hypocrisy: selling itself as a guardian of ethical AI while chasing the same contracts as everyone else. For users, this erodes trust in the one advantage OpenAI had—its perceived moral high ground.

The uninstall spike may be temporary, but the structural shifts aren’t. Regulators in the [EU](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-act) and [California](https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa) are already eyeing this as a case study in AI governance failures. If OpenAI’s military ties lead to stricter compliance costs—like mandatory audits for dual-use models—those expenses will trickle down to customers via higher subscription fees. For startups using ChatGPT’s API, that could mean [pricing hikes](https://openai.com/pricing) at a time when VC funding is drying up.

There’s a cruel irony here. OpenAI’s partnership with the Pentagon was likely intended to secure long-term revenue streams as [growth in consumer ChatGPT slows](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/05/10/openai-chatgpt-user-growth-flattens-as-competition-heats-up.html). Instead, it’s alienating the very users who fuel its valuation. The company now faces a choice: double down on military contracts and risk becoming the ‘Defense AI’ brand, or pivot back to its roots and cede ground to competitors in the enterprise space. Neither option is cost-free.

What’s clear is that the era of AI companies operating without ideological baggage is over. Every partnership, every contract, is now a political statement—and users are treating it as such. For OpenAI, the Pentagon deal wasn’t just sloppy; it was a miscalculation of how deeply its audience cares about alignment between words and actions.
