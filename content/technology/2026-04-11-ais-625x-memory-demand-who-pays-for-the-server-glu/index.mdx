Dell’s CEO [Michael Dell](https://www.dell.com/en-us/blog/authors/michael-dell/) didn’t just predict growth—he forecast a **625x surge** in AI memory demand by 2028 compared to 2022. That’s not a typo: the entire AI market’s appetite for DRAM and storage could outstrip today’s levels by nearly three orders of magnitude. For context, [NVIDIA’s H100 GPUs](https://www.nvidia.com/en-us/data-center/h100/) already guzzle 80GB of HBM3 per chip; scaling that across millions of servers explains the panic.


The problem isn’t just volume. Memory prices are volatile, and [DRAM shortages](https://www.techspot.com/news/97754-dram-prices-expected-rise-2024-due-ai-demand.html) in 2024 have already pushed costs up 13% quarter-over-quarter. Cloud providers like AWS and Azure absorb these spikes first—but history shows they [pass costs to customers](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/aws-raises-prices-for-first-time-in-a-decade/) when pressed. If Dell’s numbers hold, even mid-tier AI startups could face sticker shock by 2026.


This isn’t abstract for developers. Training a single large language model today can cost [$10M in cloud fees](https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/11/ai-training-costs-are-spiraling-out-of-control-study-finds/); multiply that by 625x memory demand, and the math gets ugly. The real bottleneck may not be GPUs after all—it’s whether the industry can even *build* enough memory chips in time.

<ArticleBreak />

The ecosystem effects ripple beyond data centers. PC gamers—already grumbling about [DRAM price hikes](https://www.pcgamer.com/hardware/dram-prices-are-rising-again-and-its-all-thanks-to-ai/)—could see their 32GB RAM kits become collateral damage in AI’s land grab. Meanwhile, hyperscalers like Google and Meta are [locking in long-term memory contracts](https://www.reuters.com/technology/google-meta-others-rush-secure-ai-chip-supply-sources-2024-01-18/), squeezing smaller players out of the market.


There’s a cruel irony here: AI’s promise of efficiency may drown in its own infrastructure costs. Startups betting on [edge AI](https://www.theverge.com/2024/3/12/24096784/ai-edge-computing-nvidia-qualcomm-intel) to cut cloud bills could find themselves priced out of the very memory needed to run local models. Even [Samsung’s aggressive DRAM expansion](https://www.anandtech.com/show/19022/samsung-to-invest-230-billion-in-new-semi-fabs-by-2042) might not keep pace if demand outstrips silicon foundries’ capacity.


For all the noise about AI’s potential, the actual story is simpler: someone’s paying for this. Whether it’s end users via higher SaaS fees, taxpayers subsidizing [CHIPS Act factories](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefing-room/statements-releases/2024/03/20/fact-sheet-biden-harris-administration-announces-8-5-billion-for-leading-edge-memory-chip-manufacturing/), or investors bankrolling unprofitable AI labs, the bill is coming due. The question isn’t if the memory will arrive—it’s who gets priced out when it doesn’t.
