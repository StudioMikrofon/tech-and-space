For years, oncologists have eyed circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) in blood as a potential early warning system for breast cancer recurrence. Now, a study presented at the [15th European Breast Cancer Conference (EBCC15)](https://www.ecco-org.eu/Events/EBCC-15) offers the strongest evidence yet—but with a critical caveat: the signal only holds for patients who underwent neoadjuvant chemotherapy before surgery.

The research, led by a team from the [Netherlands Cancer Institute](https://www.nki.nl), analyzed blood samples from 200 women with high-risk breast cancer. Unlike prior studies that tested ctDNA at diagnosis, this team drew blood *after* patients completed neoadjuvant therapy. The difference matters: women with detectable ctDNA post-treatment were **3.5 times more likely** to relapse within two years.

Yet the study’s narrow focus is its defining feature. As [Dr. Nicholas Turner](https://www.institutecancer.org.uk/research/our-researchers/dr-nicholas-turner), a ctDNA expert not involved in the work, noted, 'This isn’t a universal biomarker—it’s a tool for a specific subgroup.' The methodology excludes women who had upfront surgery or no neoadjuvant therapy, a detail often lost in broader coverage.

<ArticleBreak />

The findings, while robust within their scope, don’t yet translate to clinical practice. Current [ASCO guidelines](https://www.asco.org) don’t recommend ctDNA monitoring outside research settings, and the EBCC15 data won’t change that. The study’s **200-patient sample**, though rigorous, lacks the scale needed for regulatory approval. More critically, no randomized trial has tested whether acting on ctDNA results—say, with additional treatment—actually improves outcomes.

What the data *do* reveal is a **methodological breakthrough**: timing matters. Prior ctDNA studies faltered because tumor fragments clear from blood during treatment, masking recurrence risk. By testing *after* neoadjuvant chemo, researchers captured a more stable signal. Yet even this approach has blind spots. ctDNA can’t distinguish between residual disease and dead tumor cells shed post-therapy—a limitation the authors openly acknowledge.

For patients today, the message is unchanged: **no new tests or treatments are available**. But for researchers, the study refines the hunt for biomarkers. The next step? Larger trials pairing ctDNA monitoring with targeted interventions—work already underway in [Europe’s TREAT CTC trial](https://clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/show/NCT01548677).

For future research, the focus shifts to two questions: Can ctDNA guide *treatment decisions*, not just predictions? And can the method be adapted for patients who skip neoadjuvant therapy? The answers will determine whether this remains a research curiosity or becomes a clinical staple.
