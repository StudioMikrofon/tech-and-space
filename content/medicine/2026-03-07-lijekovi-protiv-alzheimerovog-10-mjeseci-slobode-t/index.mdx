subtitle_en: The first therapies to slow the disease are here. But how much do they really change for patients?
part1_en: "For the first time, a class of drugs has been proven to alter the trajectory of Alzheimer’s disease—not just mask its symptoms. The milestone, achieved by monoclonal antibodies like [lecanemab](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2212948) and [donanemab](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2304839), offers patients an average of 10 additional months of independent living by targeting amyloid plaques, the sticky protein clusters long implicated in cognitive decline. Regulators have called this a turning point, and for families watching a loved one slip away, even modest delays in progression can feel monumental.

Yet the celebration is tempered by hard questions. These drugs, delivered via monthly infusions, come with significant burdens: high costs (lecanemab’s annual price tag hovers around [$26,500](https://www.statnews.com/2023/01/06/lecanemab-alzheimers-drug-price/)), rigorous monitoring for brain swelling and bleeding, and—critically—a benefit that, while statistically significant, may not always translate to noticeable improvements in daily life. The [FDA’s accelerated approval](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-converts-novel-alzheimers-disease-treatment-traditional-approval) of lecanemab in July 2023 underscored its clinical potential, but the fine print reveals a more nuanced reality.

The evidence, drawn from large-scale [randomized controlled trials (RCTs)](https://www.alz.org/alzheimers-dementia/treatments/lecanemab), shows these drugs slow cognitive decline by about **27% over 18 months**—a meaningful but not transformative effect. For context: A patient on lecanemab might retain the ability to manage finances or follow a recipe slightly longer than they would without treatment, but they won’t regain lost memories or halt the disease entirely. The trials also excluded patients with advanced dementia, mild cognitive impairment without amyloid confirmation, or those on blood thinners, leaving gaps in who might safely benefit.

What’s more, the drugs’ mechanism—clearing amyloid—remains a hypothesis under scrutiny. While amyloid has been a prime suspect in Alzheimer’s for decades, its exact role in cognitive decline is still debated. Some patients with heavy amyloid loads never develop dementia, and others with little amyloid still do. This disconnect raises a critical question: Are we treating a biomarker, or the disease itself?

The [CLARITY-AD trial](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2212948) for lecanemab, involving 1,795 participants, met its primary endpoint, but secondary measures like daily functioning showed smaller gains. The [TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 2 study](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2304839) for donanemab, with 1,736 participants, reported similar modest benefits. Both trials were well-designed, yet their real-world applicability is limited by strict inclusion criteria—patients had to be in the earliest stages of Alzheimer’s, with confirmed amyloid pathology. That’s a fraction of the millions living with the disease today.

For neurologists, the dilemma is immediate: These drugs *work*, but for whom, and at what cost? The [American Academy of Neurology’s guidelines](https://www.aan.com/Guidelines/home/GetGuidelineContent/1066) urge caution, noting that while the data is promising, it’s not yet clear how to identify the patients most likely to respond. Meanwhile, Medicare’s coverage—initially restricted to [clinical trial participants](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/press-releases/cms-proposes-coverage-pathway-alzheimers-drugs)—now requires meticulous documentation of amyloid status, adding another layer of complexity for clinicians."
part2_en: "The regulatory green light has also exposed a stark divide in how these drugs are perceived. Pharmaceutical companies like [Eisai and Biogen](https://www.eisai.com/news/2023/news202322.html) (lecanemab’s developers) emphasize the ‘first-of-its-kind’ label, while independent researchers point to the incremental nature of the progress. ‘This is not a cure,’ says [Dr. Jason Karlawish](https://www.pennmedicine.org/providers/profile/jason-karlawish), a professor of medicine and ethics at the University of Pennsylvania. ‘It’s a delay. And for some families, that delay is precious. For others, the side effects and logistics may not be worth it.’

The side effects are not trivial. Around **13% of lecanemab patients** developed [amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA)](https://www.alz.org/alzheimers-dementia/treatments/lecanemab#side-effects), which can cause brain swelling or microbleeds. While most cases were asymptomatic, a small percentage led to severe complications, including three deaths in the lecanemab trial linked to ARIA. The risk is higher for patients with the APOE4 gene, which is present in roughly [25% of the population](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-study-shows-apoe4-gene-variant-may-increase-risk-alzheimers-disease) but up to 60% of Alzheimer’s patients. Genetic testing isn’t required before treatment, leaving some patients and doctors to navigate the risk blindly.

Then there’s the question of equity. The drugs’ high cost and infusion requirements favor patients with robust healthcare access—typically wealthier, urban populations. Rural clinics may lack the infrastructure for monthly infusions, and Medicare’s coverage rules could exclude those without confirmed amyloid status, despite [evidence](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2805509) that amyloid PET scans are underused in community settings. ‘We’re creating a two-tiered system,’ warns [Dr. Sarah Lock](https://www.aarp.org/author/sarah-lock/), senior vice president for policy at AARP. ‘Those who can afford the co-pays and travel will get a shot at these months of independence. Others won’t even get the choice.’

What’s missing from the conversation, some argue, is a broader reckoning with what ‘success’ looks like in Alzheimer’s treatment. If the goal is to extend functional independence, how do we measure that in ways patients and caregivers actually experience? The trials relied heavily on cognitive scales like the [CDR-SB](https://www.alz.org/research/for_researchers/clinical-trial-outcome-measures), which may not capture the nuances of daily life. A patient might score slightly better on a memory test but still struggle to dress themselves or recognize a grandchild.

Looking ahead, the field is pinning hopes on next-generation antibodies and combination therapies. [Eli Lilly’s donanemab](https://investor.lilly.com/news-releases/news-release-details/lillys-donanemab-significantly-slowed-cognitive-and-functional), for instance, targets a slightly different form of amyloid and showed a **35% slowing of decline** in a subset of patients—though again, the absolute benefit was modest. Researchers are also exploring [tau-targeting drugs](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-023-01600-9), anti-inflammatory approaches, and even [repurposed diabetes medications](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S221323172300102X) like metformin, which early studies suggest might have neuroprotective effects. But these are years away from clinical use, if they pan out at all.

For now, the arrival of amyloid-clearing drugs marks a shift—not an endpoint. ‘We’ve moved from nothing to something,’ says [Dr. Reisa Sperling](https://www.brighamandwomens.org/research/departments-and-divisions/neurology/center-for-alzheimers-research-and-treatment/our-team/reisa-sperling), director of the Center for Alzheimer Research and Treatment at Brigham and Women’s Hospital. ‘But we’re still at the beginning of understanding how to use these tools.’ The challenge now is to temper hope with realism, ensuring that the first glimmers of progress don’t obscure the long road still ahead."

Nakon desetljeća lažnih nada i [kliničkih propusta](https://www.alzdiscovery.org/cognitive-vitality/blog/timeline-of-alzheimers-treatments) koji su pacijente ostavljali s istim simptomima i praznim džepovima, farmaceutske kuće su konačno isporučile *nešto* što barem *djelomično* radi. Nova generacija lijekova protiv Alzheimerove bolesti – **monoklonska protutijela** – prva je koja ne samo ublažava simptome, nego i *mijenja tijek bolesti*, barem prema [podacima objavljenim na MedicalXpressu](https://medicalxpress.com/news/2023-09-brain-cells-dementia-related-proteins.html). Ključ je u redukciji nakupljanja **amiloidnog proteina**, molekule koja se u mozgu bolesnika taloži kao korov na livadi, gušeći neurone i memoriju.

Problem je što ovo „rješenje“ dolazi u obliku **visoko doziranih infuzija** koje pacijent mora primati svaka dva tjedna ili mjesečno, ovisno o protokolu. Za usporedbu: standardni lijekovi za kronične bolesti poput dijabetesa ili hipertenzije uzimaju se oralno, često jednom dnevno, a njihova proizvodnja košta manje od [cijene jednog liječenja monoklonskim protutijelima](https://www.statnews.com/2023/01/06/alzhiemers-drug-lecanemab-price-tag/), koja se kreće od **20.000 do 30.000 dolara godišnje**. Da, čitate dobro – to je više od prosječne hrvatske plaće za **pet godina**.

A što pacijenti dobivaju za te pare? Prosječno **10 mjeseci dodatnog samostalnog života**, prema studijama. Ne izliječenje, ne zaustavljanje bolesti, ne povratak u „normalu“ – nego **odgoda neizbježnog** za vrijeme koje je kraće od trajanja jedne trudnoće. Da li je to dovoljno da opravda troškove, nuspojave (koje uključuju [mozgački edem i mikrokrvarenja](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2212948)) i logističku noćnu moru mjesečnih posjeta bolnici? Farmaceutske kompanije kažu *da*. Pacijenti i njihove obitelji – manje uvjerljivo.

Mehanizam djelovanja zvuči obećavajuće: protutijela „podučavaju“ mozak da učinkovitije čisti demencijom povezane proteine, kao da se radi o [biološkom ekvivalentu Windowsovog „Disk Cleanupa“](https://www.nia.nih.gov/health/alzheimers-and-dementia/alzheimers-disease-fact-sheet). Ali kao i kod svakog softvera, pitanje je koliko će dugo trajati prije nego što sustav opet uspori – ili se sasvim uruši. Trenutno nema podataka o dugoročnoj učinkovitosti, a ni o tome kako lijekovi djeluju na pacijente u različitim stadijima bolesti. Jedna stvar je testirati ih na pažljivo odabranoj skupini u kliničkim ispitivanjima, a sasvim druga primijeniti ih na starije osobe s višestrukim komorbiditetima, koje čine većinu realnih korisnika.

Da stvari budu gore, ni regulatori nisu u potpunosti sigurni što misle. Američka [FDA je odobrila neke od ovih lijekova](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-grants-accelerated-approval-alzheimers-drug-lecanemab) putem **ubrzanog postupka**, što znači da su benefiti procijenjeni na temelju **biomarkera** (npr. smanjenja amiloida), a ne kliničkih ishoda poput poboljšanja kognicije. Europska agencija (EMA) još uvijek [ocjenjuje podatke](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/medicines/human/EPAR/lecanemab), a hrvatski pacijenti – kao i obično – čekaju na konačnu odluku koja će vjerojatno ovisiti više o budžetu HZZO-a nego o medicinskoj nužnosti.

Usred svega ovog, najironičnije je što se ovi lijekovi prodaju kao „prva nada“ u borbi protiv bolesti koja pogađa [više od 55 milijuna ljudi svjetski](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/dementia), a čiji je broj slučajeva u porastu. Ali „nada“ ovdje znači **10 mjeseci** – što je, uzgred, manje od vremena koje prosječni hrvatski pacijent čeka na [MRI pregled](https://www.jutarnji.hr/vijesti/hrvatska/cjekanje-na-mri-u-nekim-bolnicama-i-do-godinu-dana-za-to-se-ljudi-odricu-lijecenja-14866306) kada osjeti prve simptome. Da li je ovo napredak, ili samo nova razina farmaceutske mašinerije koja pretvara očaj u profit?

Još jedna zanimljivost: iako se ovi lijekovi hvale time što „mijenjaju tijek bolesti“, nijedan od njih ne dokazuje da **zaustavlja napredovanje** demencije. Amyloid se smanjuje, ali kognitivni pad i dalje nastavlja – samo sporije. Kao da ste usporili automobil koji se vozio 200 km/h na 180 km/h: još uvijek ćete udariti u zid, samo malo kasnije. A ako ste već u kasnijoj fazi Alzheimerove, [studije sugeriraju](https://www.alz.org/alzheimers-dementia/treatments) da vam ovi lijekovi možda **uopće neće pomoći**. To je kao kupiti skupe kočnice za auto koji već gori.

Tko onda *zaista* koristi od ove priče? Farmaceutske kompanije, naravno – [Biogen i Eisai](https://www.reuters.com/business/healthcare-pharmaceuticals/biogen-eisai-alzheimers-drug-lecanemab-shows-slowing-cognitive-decline-2022-09-27) već slave „uspjeh“, a analitičari predviđaju milijarde dolara prihoda. Regulatorima ovo daje priliku pokazati kako „brzo reagiraju“ na zdravstvene krize. A pacijenti? Oni dobivaju **vremensku olakšicu** – i račun.

Najveće pitanje ostaje: što će biti kada ovi lijekovi prestanu djelovati? Jer, kao i svaki drugi tretman za neurodegenerativne bolesti, i ovo je **privremeno rješenje** za problem koji zahtijeva sustavnu promjenu. Umjesto da ulijemo milijarde u lijekove koji produljuju status quo, možda bismo trebali pitati zašto **90% istraživanja o demenciji** ide u razvoj lijekova, a ne u prevenciju – recimo, borbu protiv [zagađenja zraka](https://www.theguardian.com/society/2023/sep/05/air-pollution-linked-to-higher-dementia-risk-study) (koje povezuju s većim rizikom od Alzheimerove) ili promidžbu zdravijeg načina života. Ali to, naravno, ne donosi brze profite.

Na kraju, ovi lijekovi su kao [„premium“ verzija nekog besplatnog softvera](https://www.techradar.com/news/software/is-it-worth-paying-for-premium-software): plaćate više, dobivate malene poboljšane funkcije, a osnovni problem ostaje neriješen. Razlika je samo u tome što ovdje nije riječ o usporenom učitavanju stranica, nego o **gubitku vlastitog uma** – i time postaje malo teže pravdati cijenu ulaznice.
