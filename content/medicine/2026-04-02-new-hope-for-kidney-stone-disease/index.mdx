Scientists at the Buck Institute for Research on Aging have made a significant discovery in the treatment of primary hyperoxaluria type 2 (PH2), a rare genetic disorder that causes progressive kidney failure in infants and young adults. The study, which used a mouse model of PH2, found that an orally administered small molecule, N-propargylglycine (N-PPG), can completely prevent the formation of calcium oxalate kidney stones. According to [MedicalXpress](https://medicalxpress.com/), this is a major breakthrough in the treatment of this devastating disease.


The researchers used a rigorous methodology to test the effectiveness of N-PPG, including [in vivo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_vivo) experiments and [histological analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histology). Their findings, which have been [peer-reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer_review), provide strong evidence for the potential of N-PPG in treating PH2.

<ArticleBreak />

While the results of this study are promising, it is essential to note that the sample size was limited to a mouse model, and further research is needed to confirm the effectiveness of N-PPG in humans. As [the National Institutes of Health](https://www.nih.gov/) notes, PH2 is a rare disease that affects an estimated 1,700 people in the US, although many more cases may go undiagnosed. The development of a treatment for this disease would have a significant impact on the lives of those affected.


The next step in the research process will be to conduct [clinical trials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_trial) to test the safety and efficacy of N-PPG in humans. This will involve [regulatory approval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulatory_agency) from agencies such as the [FDA](https://www.fda.gov/). As [the Buck Institute](https://www.buckinstitute.org/) notes, this is a critical step in bringing new treatments to market.

This means that patients with PH2 may soon have access to a new treatment option, which could significantly improve their quality of life. The development of N-PPG as a treatment for PH2 has the potential to make a major impact on the lives of those affected by this disease. Further research is needed to fully realize the potential of this treatment.
