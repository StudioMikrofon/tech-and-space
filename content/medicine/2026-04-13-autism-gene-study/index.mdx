Researchers have published a study in Nature Medicine on the genetic landscape of autism in Latin American populations. The study, which analyzed data from over 15,000 individuals, found significant overlap in deleterious coding variations associated with autism across different ancestries. This suggests that the genetic risk factors for autism may be more universal than previously thought. According to the study, the findings have implications for our understanding of the genetic basis of autism.


The study's authors note that the research was conducted using a combination of genetic and statistical analysis techniques. They also acknowledge that the study has limitations, including a relatively small sample size compared to other studies in the field. However, the study's results are [CONFIRMED](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04228-6) by the data and provide new insights into the genetic basis of autism.


As [reported](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04228-6) in Nature Medicine, the study's findings have the potential to inform the development of new treatments for autism. The study's results also highlight the importance of [diversity in genetic research](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7146853/), as the study's sample population was predominantly Latin American.
