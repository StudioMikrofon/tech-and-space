part1_en: "Every time a patient steps into a clinic, wears a fitness tracker, or logs symptoms into an app, they generate data—data that now powers a multi-billion-dollar industry. The question of who controls that data, and for what purpose, has simmered for years. But a new proposal from the [Health Data Governance Initiative](https://www.healthdatagovernance.org) reframes the debate: What if health data were treated not as a commodity, but as a regulated public utility, like water or electricity?

The idea isn’t just academic. In 2023, [STAT News reported](https://www.statnews.com/2023/11/15/health-data-public-utility-proposal/) that private companies—from tech giants like Google and Apple to specialized health analytics firms—monetize patient data in ways that often remain opaque to the individuals who generated it. The proposal argues that this model risks exploiting a resource that should serve collective health, not corporate balance sheets. But the tension is deeper than ownership: it’s about whether data’s primary role should be improving care or fueling innovation.

At its core, the debate hinges on two competing visions. The first, championed by patient advocacy groups, sees health data as a *public good*—a shared asset that should be governed by transparency, consent, and equitable access. The second, dominant in Silicon Valley and parts of the healthcare industry, treats data as a *market asset*, where value is extracted through analysis, AI training, or targeted services. Neither vision is inherently wrong, but their collision raises urgent questions: Can data serve both purposes? And if not, which takes precedence?

The proposal’s authors, a coalition of policy researchers and ethicists, point to existing models. Estonia’s [national health data system](https://e-estonia.com/solutions/healthcare/healthcare-data-exchange/), for instance, treats patient records as a public resource while allowing controlled private-sector access. Similarly, the UK’s NHS has experimented with [data trusts](https://www.health.org.uk/publications/long-reads/using-data-trusts-to-improve-health-and-care) to balance innovation with patient protection. But these examples also reveal a critical challenge: even well-intentioned frameworks struggle with enforcement, scalability, and the sheer volume of data generated outside traditional healthcare settings.

What’s often missing from the conversation is the *clinical* perspective. For physicians, data utility isn’t about monetization—it’s about interoperability. "We spend more time chasing down records than analyzing them," Dr. [Atul Gawande](https://www.ariadnelabs.org/team/atul-gawande/) noted in a 2022 interview with *The New Yorker*. If data were standardized and accessible, the argument goes, care coordination would improve, errors would decline, and research could accelerate. But that vision requires infrastructure and incentives that current systems—public or private—haven’t delivered."
part2_en: "The counterargument from industry is predictable: regulation stifles innovation. Tech companies argue that their ability to aggregate and analyze data at scale has already led to breakthroughs, from [AI-assisted diagnostics](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06558-1) to personalized treatment algorithms. A 2023 [McKinsey report](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/healthcare/our-insights/how-big-data-and-ai-are-transforming-healthcare) estimated that AI-driven data analytics could save the U.S. healthcare system $360 billion annually by 2030—if allowed to operate without excessive constraints.

Yet the trade-offs aren’t just economic. When data is treated as a private asset, patients often lose sight of how it’s used. A [2022 *JAMA* study](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2798772) found that 70% of health apps share user data with third parties, frequently without explicit consent. The consequences aren’t abstract: in 2021, [Flo Health](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2021/01/ftc-alleges-popular-fertility-app-shared-health-data-third-parties-after-promising-keep-it) settled with the FTC over allegations it shared sensitive reproductive health data with Facebook and Google, despite promising users privacy. Incidents like these underscore why the "public utility" model resonates—it’s not just about access, but about trust.

The proposal’s most radical suggestion is a *data dividend*: a system where individuals or communities receive compensation when their aggregated data generates profit. This isn’t without precedent. California’s [Consumer Privacy Act](https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa) includes limited data valuation mechanisms, though enforcement has been uneven. Critics argue that such models could incentivize data hoarding or create bureaucratic hurdles, but proponents counter that the status quo—where patients bear the risks of data misuse while corporations reap the rewards—is far worse.

So what would a utility model actually look like? The [proposal outlines](https://www.healthdatagovernance.org/whitepaper) three pillars: **standardization** (common formats for interoperability), **stewardship** (independent oversight bodies), and **equitable access** (prioritizing public health and research over commercial use). The devil, of course, is in the details. Who defines "equitable"? How are oversight bodies funded and held accountable? And what happens to the thousands of startups and research projects that rely on current data flows?

For patients, the immediate impact might be limited. Most people won’t notice a shift in how their data is managed—unless something goes wrong. But the long-term stakes are profound. If data becomes a regulated utility, it could accelerate research into rare diseases, reduce disparities in care, and curb the power of tech monopolies. If it remains a commodity, the benefits of big data may continue to accrue unevenly, with innovation concentrated in profitable areas like chronic disease management while neglected conditions languish.

The conversation is no longer theoretical. The EU’s [Health Data Space](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/european-health-data-space) regulation, set to take effect in 2025, will test some of these principles in practice. Meanwhile, in the U.S., the [ONC’s information blocking rules](https://www.healthit.gov/topic/laws-regulation-and-policy/information-blocking) and HIPAA updates are inching toward greater patient control. The question isn’t whether health data will be reimagined—but who gets to decide how."

Ako ste ikad mislili da su vaši podaci o zdravlju *vaši*, dobrodošli u klub naivnih optimista. U stvarnosti, dok vi plaćate pretplatu na aplikaciju za praćenje krvi pod tlaka, neka poduzeća već dugo pretvaraju vaše laboratorijske nalaze, receptne historije i čak podatke o snu u [zlato 21. stoljeća](https://www.statnews.com/2023/11/15/patient-health-data-public-utility-arpa-h/). Najnoviji prijedlog – da se zdravstveni podaci tretiraju kao **javna usluga**, poput vode ili struje – dolazi od nekadašnjeg šefa podataka u [ARPA-H](https://arpa-h.gov/), agenciji koja je 2022. dobila **2,5 milijardi dolara** da rješava upravo takve *malih, nevažnih* problema.

Problem je star koliko i digitalizacija medicine, ali sada dobiva novi okvir: umjesto da podaci budu roba koju prodaju [Epic](https://www.epic.com/), [Google Health](https://health.google/) ili Apple, trebali bi biti regulirani kao infrastruktura – s pravilima o pristupu, cijeni i transparentnosti. **Dr. Michael Stebbins**, bivši direktor za podatke u ARPA-H, ne sugerira ovo iz dobrote srca, već zato što je vidio kako se isti podaci koji bi mogli spasiti živote pretvaraju u [profitne modele za umjetnu inteligeniju](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-023-02390-0). Ironično, dok se tech kompanije hvale kako će njihovi algoritmi *transformirati* medicinu, istovremeno blokiraju pristup sirovim podacima koji bi omogućili neovisna istraživanja.

Danas pacijent koji želi preuzeti vlastite medicinske zapise često nailazi na labirint administrativnih prepreka, skupe naknade za *obradu zahtjeva* ili – najčešće – **PDF-ove koji izgledaju kao da su ispisani na pisaćem stroju 1998.** A istovremeno, iste te podatke kompanije kao što je [Optum](https://www.optum.com/) prodaju osiguravačima, farmaceutskim divovima ili startupima za *personaliziranu medicinu* (koja, naravno, nikad nije pristaćena kao što je obećano). Prijedlog o javnoj usluzi ne bi značio nacionalizaciju podataka, već **regulaciju koja bi onemogućila monopole** – kao što reguliramo cijenje struje kako ne bi plaćali 10 kuna po kilovatu samo zato što je HEP jedini igrač u gradu.

Ali, naravno, tu je i pitanje **tko će to platiti**. Tech kompanije već godinama lobiraju protiv sličnih ideja, a bolnice – koje su već preopterećene [cyber-napadima](https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/health-care-sector-cybersecurity.pdf) i nedostatkom osoblja – ne žure se dodatno komplicirati život. ARPA-H, iako bogata, nije regulatorno tijelo, pa bi promjena zahtijevala kongresne bitke koje traju duže od pandemije. A dok čekamo, [Apple Watch](https://www.apple.com/watch/) već mjeri vašu razinu kisika u krvi i šalje podatke *negođekome* – samo ne vama u obradivom formatu.

Najzanimljivije u ovom prijedlogu nije sama ideja – koja, usput, **nije nova** (EU je već godinama eksperimentira s [Zdravstvenim podacima kao javnim dobrima](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ehealth)) – već **ko ju je pokrenuo**. ARPA-H nije neka lijeva NVO, već agencija koja je nastala pod Trumpovom administracijom (da, [ta ista](https://www.nih.gov/about-nih/what-we-do/advanced-research-projects-agency-health-arpa-h)) i koja ima mandat za *visoko-rizične* inovacije. Ako i oni kažu da je trenutni model neodrživ, možda je vrijeme da prestanemo vjerovati kako će [Google DeepMind](https://deepmind.google/) sam od sebe odlučiti dijeliti podatke s javnošću.

Prijedlog o javnoj usluzi ima tri ključne prednosti: prvo, **demokratizira pristup** – istraživači iz manjih ustanova ne bi više morali moliti [Epic](https://www.epic.com/) za pristup podacima koji su, teorijski, već javni. Drugo, **smanjuje troškove** – bolnice danas plaćaju milijune za EHR sustave koji su zatvoreni kao Fort Knox, a pacijenti plaćaju dvostruko: jednom porezima za javno zdravstvo, a drugim putem pretplata na *wellness* aplikacije koje prodaju iste podatke natrag sustavu. Treće, **stavlja granice monopolima** – jer ako je nešto regulirana usluga, ne možeš samo tako odbiti pristup konkurentu ili naplatiti 10.000 eura za API ključ.

Naravno, ima i zamki. **Tko će odrediti cijene?** Ako je zdravstveni podatak javna usluga, hoće li ga država subvencionirati, ili će pacijenti opet platiti preko poreza? I, najvažnije: **ko će biti odgovoran kada podaci procure?** [Optum](https://www.optum.com/) je 2022. imao [provalu podataka](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/breach-notification/index.html) koja je pogodila 11 milijuna ljudi – ako je to moguće u privatnom sektoru, što će se dogoditi kada podaci budu *dostupniji*? ARPA-H kaže da bi rješenje mogao biti **decentralizirani model**, poput onog kod [blockchain zdravstvenih zapisa](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7337253/), ali to zvuči kao pokušaj da se problem *tech-brozima* riješi… još više tehnologijom.

Na kraju, pitanje nije samo *hoćemo li* regulirati zdravstvene podatke, već **koje će interese ta regulacija služiti**. Ako završimo s modelom gdje država plaća tech kompanijama za *upravljanje* podacima (kao što sada plaćamo [Palantiru](https://www.palantir.com/) za vojne analize), bit ćemo u istom, samo skupljem, paklu. A ako pacijenti i dalje neće moći lako preuzeti svoje podatke u mašinski čitljivom formatu, sve ovo je samo **još jedna priča o tome kako nam neko drugdje objašnjava što je najbolje za nas** – dok nam u međuvremenu [Meta](https://about.fb.com/news/) prodaje reklame na temelju onoga što smo jeli za ručak.
