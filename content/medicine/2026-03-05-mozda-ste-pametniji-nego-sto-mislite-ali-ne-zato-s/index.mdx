For decades, neuroscience has operated under a simple assumption: the brain gets more efficient by cutting redundancy. Like a well-oiled machine, learning was thought to streamline neural activity—fewer signals, less repetition, faster responses. But a [study published in *Science*](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adg8991) from the University of Rochester’s Del Monte Institute for Neuroscience flips that script. The research suggests that mastery isn’t about *fewer* neurons firing, but about *better coordination*—a shift from solo performers to a synchronized ensemble.

The finding emerges from experiments tracking neural activity in monkeys as they learned to distinguish subtle visual patterns. Rather than seeing individual neurons quiet down (the expected sign of efficiency), the team observed something counterintuitive: neurons in the visual cortex began firing *more in unison*, sharing information like a network fine-tuning its connections. Lead author [Jude Mitchell](https://www.urmc.rochester.edu/people/26127731-jude-f-mitchell), an assistant professor of neuroscience, compares it to a jazz band: ‘Early on, each musician plays their part independently. With practice, they start listening to each other, anticipating cues, and the music becomes tighter—not because anyone’s playing faster, but because they’re aligned.’

This challenges a foundational idea in cognitive science: that expertise is primarily about *speed*. The classic example is a chess grandmaster scanning a board and instantly recognizing patterns—a skill long attributed to rapid neural processing. But if this study holds, the advantage might lie less in raw speed and more in how neurons *collaborate*. It’s a distinction with implications far beyond lab monkeys. For patients recovering from strokes or traumatic brain injuries, where neural pathways must relearn coordination, this could reframe how we think about rehabilitation. And for AI researchers modeling human learning, it suggests that efficiency isn’t just about pruning ‘noise’—it’s about teaching systems to *integrate* signals, not just suppress them.

Yet before rewriting textbooks, it’s worth noting the study’s scope. The work focused on a single sensory task (visual discrimination) in a small cohort of macaques. While the [Del Monte Institute](https://www.urmc.rochester.edu/del-monte-institute-for-neuroscience.aspx) is a leader in translational neuroscience, this is still *basic research*—a critical piece of the puzzle, but not yet a clinical tool. The team also acknowledges that coordination might look different in other brain regions or tasks. ‘We’re not saying speed never matters,’ Mitchell clarifies. ‘We’re saying it’s not the whole story.’

To understand why this matters, consider two real-world scenarios where neural coordination could play a role. First, *dyslexia*: some theories link reading difficulties to ‘noisy’ visual processing, where letters appear to jump or blur. If learning to read relies on neurons synchronizing—not just firing faster—could interventions target that synchronization? The study doesn’t answer this, but it opens a door. Second, *aging*: older adults often complain of slower processing, but their struggle might not be pure speed. If coordination falters with age, therapies could focus on restoring neural ‘teamwork’ rather than just trying to accelerate signals.

The study’s methodology also raises questions about how we measure learning. Most cognitive training programs (like [Lumosity](https://www.lumosity.com/) or [BrainHQ](https://www.brainhq.com/)) emphasize speed and accuracy as proxies for improvement. But if coordination is key, those metrics might miss the mark. ‘We’ve been optimizing for the wrong thing,’ says [Earl Miller](https://picower.mit.edu/people/earl-k-miller), a neuroscientist at MIT not involved in the study. ‘It’s like judging a symphony by how fast the violins play, not how well they harmonize.’ Miller’s lab has [previously shown](https://www.nature.com/articles/nn.2216) that working memory relies on neural rhythms synchronizing across brain regions—a finding that aligns with this new work.

So what’s the catch? For starters, the study is *observational*: it describes a pattern but doesn’t prove causation. It’s possible that coordination is a byproduct of learning, not its driver. The team also didn’t test whether artificially *disrupting* coordination would impair performance—a critical next step. And while the monkey model is robust for visual tasks, human cognition involves layers of abstraction (language, planning) that may not follow the same rules. ‘This is a beautiful demonstration in a controlled setting,’ says [Lisa Feldman Barrett](https://lisafeldmanbarrett.com/), a psychologist at Northeastern University. ‘But the brain is a messy, adaptive system. We need to see if this holds in more complex, real-world learning.’

Regulatory and clinical implications are even further off. No therapy or diagnostic tool has emerged from this yet, and the [FDA’s standards](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/digital-health-center-excellence/software-precertification-pilot-program) for neural-intervention devices remain stringent. For now, the study’s value lies in its challenge to orthodoxy—a reminder that the brain’s efficiency might be more about *collaboration* than *compression*.

That said, the research arrives at a moment when neuroscience is increasingly focused on *networks* over individual neurons. Projects like the [Human Brain Project](https://www.humanbrainproject.eu/) and the [NIH’s BRAIN Initiative](https://braininitiative.nih.gov/) are mapping how regions communicate, not just how they fire. This study adds weight to that shift, suggesting that the next frontier in understanding intelligence—artificial or human—might lie in decoding not just *what* neurons do, but *how they do it together*.
